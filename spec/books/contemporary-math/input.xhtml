Introduction
class="introduction"

In the months preceding an election, news outlets report on the results of political polling every day. These polls purport to let the public know which candidate is currently “leading;” generally, that’s the candidate who would win if the election were held on the day the polling data were collected. How can they be so certain that a particular candidate is in the lead without asking every voter? Then, on the night of the election, news organizations will “declare” a winner before all the votes are even counted (and sometimes with a different candidate leading among the votes that have been counted). What gives them the confidence to make those predictions? In both cases, they’re using statistics to make informed decisions based on incomplete information. In this chapter, we’ll explore some of those techniques so that we can be better consumers of information.

When a polling organization wants to try to establish which candidate will win an upcoming election, their first steps are to write the questions for their survey and to choose which people will be asked to respond to the survey questions. These can seem like simple steps, but they have far-reaching implications in the analysis the pollsters will later carry out. There are many ways to get those wrong! Before we discuss those implications, let’s define some terms to make that discussion easier.

Data are observations that are made on units. Units can be people, animals, objects, experiments, or anything else that can be measured or surveyed. Data can be measurements taken with a scientific instrument, responses to a question, or any other property of a unit that the researcher can observe or measure. Data can be numerical (quantitative data, like a person’s daily calorie intake, or the electrical resistance in a circuit), but can also simply identify a group that the unit belongs to (categorical data, like a response to a yes-or-no question, or the treatment a person receives in a pharmaceutical trial). The collection of all the units that we’re interested in is called the population, while the group of units from which we actually collect data is called a sample.

The process by which samples are chosen can strongly affect the data that are collected. Two of the most famous failures of good sampling occurred in the first half of the twentieth century.

The Literary Digest was among the most respected magazines of the early twentieth century. Despite the name, the Digest was a weekly newsmagazine. Starting in 1916, the Digest conducted a poll to try to predict the winner of each US Presidential election. For the most part, their results were good; they correctly predicted the outcome of all five elections between 1916 and 1932. In 1936, the incumbent President Franklin Delano Roosevelt faced Kansas governor Alf Landon, and once again the Digest ran their famous poll, with results published the week before the election.

Their conclusion? Landon would win in a landslide, 57% to 43%. Once the actual votes had been counted, though, Roosevelt ended up with 61% of the popular vote, 18% more than the poll predicted. What went wrong? The short answer is that the people who were chosen to receive the survey (over ten million of them!) were not a good representation of the population of voting adults. The sample was chosen using their own base of subscribers as well as publicly-available lists of people that were likely adults (and therefore eligible to vote), mostly phone books and vehicle registration records. The pollsters then mailed every single person on these lists a survey. Around a quarter of those surveys were returned; this constituted the sample that was used to make the Digest's disastrously incorrect prediction. What the pollsters failed to account for was that this election happened in the depths of the Great Depression. Only the wealthy had the disposable income to spend on telephone lines, automobiles, and magazine subscriptions, which meant only the wealthy were sent the Digest's survey. Since Roosevelt was extremely popular among poorer voters, many of Roosevelt’s supporters were excluded from the Digest's sample! (Another, more complicated factor was the low response rate; as we saw above, around 25% of the surveys were returned. This created what’s called a non-response bias. You can read more about this in the article “Why the 1936 Literary Digest Poll Failed” by Peverill Squire, published in Public Opinion Quarterly (vol. 52:125-133, 1988).)

Public opinion of the Digest was so shattered that circulation plummeted; the last issue was published just over a year later.

Sampling and Gathering Data

This example highlights the need for what is now considered the most important criterion for sampling: randomness. This randomness can be achieved in several ways. Here are some of the most common:

  • A simple random sample is chosen in a way that every unit in the population has an equal chance of being selected, and the chances of a unit being selected do not depend on the units already chosen. An example of this is choosing a group of people by drawing names out of a hat (assuming the names are well-mixed in the hat).
  • A systematic random sample is selected from an ordered list of the population (often, names sorted alphabetically, or students listed by student ID). First, we decide what proportion of the population will be in our sample. We want to express that proportion as a fraction with 1 in the numerator (for the sake of having a concrete example, let’s say we want to sample 2% of the population; 2%=210=1502%=210=150; if the number in the denominator isn’t a whole number, we can just round it off. This part of the process doesn’t have to be precise). Let’s call that number DD. Next, we’ll choose a random number between one and DD (I used the random number generator in my calculator to find a random number between 1 and 50; it gave me 31). The unit at that position will go into our sample. We’ll find the rest of our sample by choosing every DthDthunit in the list, starting with our random number (in our example, our sample would then be the units in the list at positions 31, 31+50=8131+50=81, 81+50=13181+50=131, and so forth).
  • A stratified sample is one chosen so that particular groups in the population are certain to be represented. Let’s say you are studying the population of students in a large high school (where the grades run from 9th9th to 12th12th), and you want to choose a sample of 12 students. If you use a simple or systematic random sample, there’s a pretty good chance that you’ll miss one grade completely (this will happen around 12% of the time, in fact). So, you can first divide the population into groups (the strata), then take a random sample within each stratum (that’s the singular form of “strata”). In the high school example, we could divide the population into grades, then take a random sample of three students within each grade. That would get us to the 12 students we need.
  • A cluster sample is a sample where clusters of units are chosen at random, instead of choosing individual units. For example, if we need a sample of college students, we may take a list of all the course sections being offered at the college, choose three of them at random (the sections are the clusters), and then survey all the students in those sections. A sample like this one has the advantage of convenience: if the survey needs to be administered in person, you’ll have a bunch of your sample units in one place at the same time.

    Let’s summarize these in a definition.

A simple random sample is chosen in a way that every unit in the population has an equal chance of being selected, and the chances of a unit being selected do not depend on the units already chosen.

A systematic random sample is selected from an ordered list of the population by choosing the first unit for the sample at random and then skipping ahead in the list a fixed distance to find each successive unit.

A stratified sample is a sample that is not chosen from the full population, but instead a series of smaller samples are chosen from subgroups of the population. These small samples are then put together to create the full sample.

A cluster sample is a sample where clusters of units are chosen at random, instead of choosing individual units. Every unit in the selected clusters is then a part of the sample.

To decide which type of random sample is being used in each of these, we need to focus on how the randomization is being incorporated.

  1. The surveys are being given to households, so households are the units in this case. But households aren’t being chosen randomly! Instead, streets are being chosen at random. These form clusters of units, so this is a cluster random sample.
  2. In this case, the administrator isn’t selecting patients at random from the entire list of patients. Instead, she is choosing at random from the patients who were in each of the departments (ICU, pediatrics, general) separately. The departments form strata, so this is a stratified random sample.
  3. The engineer is testing whether the phones are faulty, so those are the units. But the random process is being used to select the crates of phones. Those crates form clusters, so this is a cluster random sample.
  4. The reporter isn’t using a random process at all, so this sample doesn’t belong to any of the types we have been talking about (none of these). A sample like this one is sometimes described as a convenience sample, and shouldn’t be used in a statistical setting.
  5. The executive is choosing her sample completely at random from the full population, so this is a simple random sample.
  6. The agent is choosing from the full population, but is only choosing the first unit for the sample at random; the rest are chosen by skipping down the list systematically. Thus, this is a systematic random sample.

George Gallup

George Gallup (1901 – 1984) rose to fame in 1936 when his prediction of the outcome of that year’s US Presidential election was much better than the one published in Literary Digest, and he did so using a sample that was much smaller than the Digest. He even took it one step farther, predicting with high accuracy the erroneous results of the Literary Digest poll! Gallup’s theories on public opinion polling essentially created that field. In 1948, Gallup’s reputation took a bit of a hit, when he famously predicted that Thomas Dewey would beat incumbent Harry Truman in that year’s Presidential election. Over the following decades, public trust in Gallup’s polls steadily increased. The company Gallup founded continues to conduct daily public opinion polls, as well as consulting services for businesses (find it at Gallup).

Organizing Data

Once data have been collected, we turn our attention to analysis. Before we analyze, though, it’s useful to reorganize the data into a format that makes the analysis easier. For example, if our data were collected using a paper survey, our raw data are all broken down by respondent (represented by an individual response sheet). To do our analysis on all the responses to an individual question, we need to get all the responses to each question grouped together and organized for analysis. The way we organize the data depends on the type of data we’ve collected. Recall that there are two broad types of data: categorical and quantitative. We’ll look at each in turn.

Categorical Data Organization

For categorical data, the best way to organize is using a categorical frequency distribution.

categorical frequency distribution is a table with two columns. The first contains all the categories present in the data, each listed once. The second contains the frequencies of each category, which are just a count of how often each category appears in the data.

Frequency analysis has been used to identify the authors of literary works. This statistical analysis of the written word is called stylometry. Take, for example, the Federalist Papers, a series of essays jointly written by Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay. Since all of the essays were originally published anonymously, the true identity of the author of each was not always known. However, for those essays whose authorship was disputed, statisticians have been able to make educated guesses based on analysis of the frequency of certain words (for example, Madison is known to use the word “by” far more often than Hamilton). More recently, a data scientist named Dr. Patrick Juola wrote a program that does a more advanced version of the analysis described above. His program correctly inferred that the novel The Cuckoo’s Calling, which had been attributed to a military veteran named Robert Galbraith, had in fact been written by J.K. Rowling, author of the Harry Potter novels!

Quantitative Data

If the data we have collected are quantitative, we have a couple of options for organization, depending on the responses. If there are just a few possible responses, we can create a frequency distribution just like the ones we made for categorical data above. For example, if we’re surveying a group of high school students and we ask for each student’s age, we’ll likely only get whole-number responses between 13 and 19. Since there are only around seven (and likely fewer) possible responses, a frequency distribution like those above is appropriate.

If there are many possible responses, a frequency distribution table like the ones we’ve seen so far isn’t really useful; there will likely be a whole bunch of responses with a frequency of one, which means the table will be no better than looking at the raw data. In these cases, we can create a binned frequency distribution. A binned frequency distribution groups the possible responses into ranges of values called bins, then records the number of responses in each bin. For example, if we have physiometric data including heights in centimeters, we might create bins like 150-155, 155-160, and so forth (making sure that every data value falls into a bin). We must be careful, though; in the example above, it’s not clear which bin would contain a response of 155 cm. Usually, responses on the edge of a bin are placed in the higher bin, but it’s good practice to make that clear. Another way to handle that issue in cases where the responses are rounded off is to leave a gap between the bins that couldn’t contain any responses. In our example, if the measurements were all rounded off to the nearest centimeter, we could make bins like 150-154, 155-159, etc. (since a response like 154.2 isn’t possible). We’ll use that method going forward. How do we decide what the boundaries of our bins should be? There’s no one right way to do that, but there are some guidelines that can be helpful:

  1. Every data value should fall into exactly one bin. For example, if the lowest value in our data is 42, the lowest bin should not be 45-49.
  2. Every bin should have the same width. Note that if we shift the upper limits of our bins down a bit to avoid ambiguity like we described above, we can’t simply subtract the lower limit from the upper limit to get the bin width; instead, we subtract the lower limit of the bin from the lower limit of the next bin. For example, if we’re looking at GPAs rounded to the nearest hundredth, we might choose bins like 2.00-2.24, 2.25-2.49, 2.50-2.74, etc. These bins all have width 0.25.
  3. If the minimum or maximum value of the data fall right on the boundary between two bins, then it’s OK to bend the rule just a little in order to avoid having an additional bin containing just that one value. We’ll see an example of this in just a moment.
  4. If we have too many or too few bins, it can be difficult to get a good sense of the distribution. Seven or eight bins is ideal, but that’s not a firm rule. Anything between five and twelve is fine; we’ll often choose the number of bins so that the widths are nice round numbers.

Summarizing raw data is the first step we must take when we want to communicate the results of a study or experiment to a broad audience. Reading things like the frequency tables we generated in the last module can be difficult, though; if the table is large, it can be tough to compare the first row to the last row. As the old saying goes, though: a picture is worth a thousand words (or, in this case, summary statistics)! Just as our techniques for organizing data depended on the type of data we were looking at, the methods we’ll use for creating visualizations will vary. Let’s start by considering categorical data.

Visualizing Categorical Data: Bar Charts and Pie Charts

If the data we’re visualizing is categorical, then we want a quick way to represent graphically the relative numbers of units that fall in each category. When we created the frequency distributions in the last module, all we did was count the number of units in each category and record that number (this was the frequency of that category). Frequencies are nice when we’re organizing and summarizing data; they’re easy to compute, and they’re always whole numbers. But they can be difficult for an outsider who’s being introduced to your data. Let’s consider a quick example. Suppose you surveyed some people and asked for their favorite color. You communicate your results using a frequency distribution. Now Jerry comes along and wants to know about favorite colors, so Jerry reads your frequency distribution. The first row shows that twelve people indicated that green was their favorite color. That’s interesting, but Jerry has no way of knowing if that’s a lot of people without knowing how many people total took your survey. Twelve is a pretty significant number of people if only twenty-five took the survey, but it’s next to nothing if you recorded a thousand responses. For that reason, we will often summarize categorical data not with frequencies, but with proportions.

The proportion of data that fall into a particular category is computed by dividing the frequency for that category by the total number of units in the data. Proportions can be expressed as fractions, decimals, or percentages.

Now, let’s turn to visualizations. There are two primary visualizations that we’ll use for categorical data: bar charts and pie charts.

A bar chart is a visualization of categorical data that consists of a series of rectangles arranged side-by-side (but not touching). Each rectangle corresponds to one of the categories. All of the rectangles have the same width. The height of each rectangle corresponds to either the number of units in the corresponding category or the proportion of the total units that fall into the category. A pie chart is a visualization that consists of a circle divided into wedges, with each wedge corresponding to a category. The proportion of the area of the entire circle that each wedge represents corresponds to the proportion of the data in that category.

Both of these representations of our data work on the same principle: if proportions are represented as areas, then it’s easy to compare two proportions by comparing the corresponding areas. Let’s build a bar chart first.

Florence Nightingale

Florence Nightingale (1820-1910) is best remembered today for her contributions in the medical field; after witnessing the horrors of field hospitals which tended to the wounded during the Crimean War, she championed reforms that encouraged sanitary conditions in hospitals. For those efforts, she is today considered the founder of modern nursing.

Nightingale is also remembered for her contributions in statistics, especially in the ways we visualize data. She developed a version of the pie chart today known as a polar area diagram, which she used to visualize the causes of death among the soldiers in the war, highlighting the number of preventable deaths the British Army suffered in that conflict.

In 1859, the Royal Statistical Society honored her for her contributions to the discipline by electing her to join that organization. She was the first woman to be so honored. She was later named an honorary member of the American Statistical Association.

Visualizing Quantitative Data: Histograms

There are a several good ways to visualize quantitative data. In this section, we’ll talk about two types: stem-and-leaf plots and histograms. Another type (the box plot) will have to wait until we’ve learned about a couple of other techniques for describing data.

Stem-and-Leaf Plots

Stem-and-leaf plots are visualization tools that fall somewhere between a list of all the raw data and a graph. These plots are really nothing more than a fancy way of listing out all the raw data; as a result, they shouldn’t be used to visualize large datasets. Let’s take a look at how to build and read these plots, after a definition.

A stem-and-leaf plot is a visualization of quantitative data. It consists of a list of stems on the left and the corresponding leaves on the right, separated by a line. The stems are the numbers that make up the data only up to the next-to-last digit, and the leaves are the final digits. There is one leaf for every data value (which means that leaves may be repeated), and the leaves should be evenly-spaced across all stems.

This is one of those definitions that’s difficult to understand without referencing an example, so let’s first look at how to read a stem-and-leaf plot.

Stem-and-leaf plots are useful in that they give us a sense of the shape of the data. Are the data evenly spread out over the stems, or are some stems “heavier” with leaves? Are the heavy stems on the low side, the high side, or somewhere in the middle? These are questions about the distribution of the data.

The distribution of quantitative data describes how the variables are spread out over its range. Some words we use to describe distributions are uniform (data are equally distributed across the range), symmetric (data are bunched up in the middle, then taper off in the same way above and below the middle), left-skewed (data are bunched up at the high end, and taper off toward the low end), and right-skewed (data are bunched up at the low end, and taper off toward the high end).

Looking back at the stem-and-leaf plot in Example 9, we can see that the data are bunched up at the low end and taper off toward the high end; that set of data is right-skewed. Knowing the distribution of a set of data gives us useful information about the property that the data are measuring.

As we mentioned above, stem-and-leaf plots aren’t always going to be useful. For example, if all the data in your data set are between 20 and 29, then you’ll just have one stem, which isn’t terribly useful (there are methods for addressing this called stem splitting, which we won’t go into). Finally, if you have dozens or hundreds (or more) data values, then a stem-and-leaf plot becomes too unwieldy to be useful. Fortunately, we have other tools we can use.

Histograms

Histograms are visualizations that can be used for any set of quantitative data, no matter how big or spread out. Unlike with stem-and-leaf plots, though, we cannot recreate the original data set from just a histogram. However, histograms are easy to make with technology, and are great for identifying the distribution of our data.

A histogram is a type of bar chart for quantitative data. Histograms are built using a binned frequency distribution. They differ from a categorical bar chart in that the horizontal axis is labeled with numbers (not ranges of numbers), and the bars are drawn so that they touch each other. The heights of the bars reflect the frequencies in each bin.

Learning Objectives

After completing this module, you should be able to:

  1. Calculate mode of a data set
  2. Calculate the median of a data set
  3. Calculate the mean of a data set
  4. Contrast measures of central tendency to identify most representative average
  5. Solve application problems involving mean, median and mode

What exactly do we mean when we describe something as “average?” Is the height of an average person the height that more people share than any other? Could it be better described this way: Line up every person in the world, in order from shortest to tallest, then find the person right in the middle. Is that person’s height the average? Or maybe it’s something more esoteric: Imagine a game where you and a friend are trying to guess the typical person’s height. Once the guesses are made, you bring in every person and measure their height. You and your friend then figure out how far off each of your guesses were from the actual value, then square that number. The result is the number of points you earn for that person. After we check every height and award points accordingly, the person with the lower score wins (because a lower score means that person’s guess was, overall, closer to the actual values). The person whose guess comes closest to the person’s true height wins. Could we define the average height to be the number that you should guess to give you the smallest possible score? These are all methods of measuring centrality (or central tendency). Centrality is just a word that describes the middle of a set of data. All give potentially different results, and all are good for different reasons. In this module, we’ll explore each of these methods of finding the average.

The Mode

In our discussion of average heights, the first possible definition we offered was the height that more people share than any other. This is the mode.

The mode of a set of quantitative data is the value that appears most often. If more than one value ties for the most, then each of those are modes. If there are two modes, the data are called bimodal.

The Median

Looking again at our example of trying to identify the height of the “average” person, our second attempt involved lining everyone up in order by height and finding the person right in the middle; that person’s height is called the median.

The median of a set of quantitative data is a number with the property that no more than 50% of the data values are less than the median and no more than 50% of the data values are greater than the median.

This is one of those definitions that is more complicated than the concept it’s trying to describe (that’s to account for some technicalities that we don’t need to worry about), so let’s look at a really simple example. Consider the list of numbers 11, 12, 13, 13, 14. Look at the first number on the list: 11. Is it the median? Well, there are no values less than 11 (that’s 0%), and there are four values greater than 11 (that’s 80%). Since more than 50% of the data are greater than 11, the definition is violated and so it’s not the median. Here’s a chart with the rest of the data, with red shading to show where the definition is violated:

Only 13 has no violations, so it’s the median. That’s how the definition works, but as I mentioned above: this definition is really more complicated than the concept it’s describing. In practice, we find the median just like we described in the average height example above: by lining up all the data values in order from smallest to largest and picking the value in the middle. For our easy example (with data values 11, 12, 13, 13, 14), that first 13 is right in the middle; there are two values to the left and two values to the right. If there’s not one value right in the middle, we pick the two closest, then choose the number exactly between them. For example, let’s say we have the data 41, 44, 46, 53. Since there are an even number of data values in our list, we can’t pick the one right in the middle. The two closest to the middle are 44 and 46, so we’ll choose the number halfway between those to be the median: 45. As this example shows, the median (unlike the mode) doesn’t have to be a number in our original set of data!

In the examples we’ve looked at so far, it’s been pretty easy to identify which number is right in the middle. If we had a very large dataset, though, it might be harder. Fortunately, we have some formulas to help us with that.

Suppose we have a set of data with n values, ordered from smallest to largest. If n is odd, then the median is the data value at position n+12n+12. If n is even, then we find the values at positions n2n2 and n2+1n2+1. If those values are named a and b, then the median is defined to be a+b2a+b2.

The Mean

The final method we suggested for identifying the “average” height was the most complicated. It involved a game where the player guesses a height, then figures out how far off that guess is from every single person’s height. Those differences get squared and added together to get a score. Our next measure of centrality gives the lowest possible score. Verifying that fact is way, way beyond what we are covering in this book. Fortunately, though, we don’t have to work all that hard to compute this measure: the mean.

Given a data set containing n total values, the mean of the data set is defined as the sum of all the data values, divided by n.

This is a computation you have likely done before. In many places, including spreadsheet programs like Microsoft Excel and Google Sheets, this number is called the average. For statisticians, though, the word average has too many possible meanings, so they prefer the one we’ll use: mean.

Which is Better: Mode, Median, or Mean?

That’s a reasonable question. If all three of these things purport to measure the same thing (centrality), why do we need all three? The answer is complicated, as they all have their own strengths and weaknesses. The mode is really easy to compute, but it may not be unique (or, if no data value appears more than once, the mode is entirely unhelpful). As for the mean and median, the main difference between them is how each is affected by extreme values. Consider this example: the mean and median of 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 are both 3. But if we change that 5 to 10, the median is still 3, but the mean is now 4. Because of this, we say that the mean is sensitive to extreme values, while the median isn’t. That can help us decide which of the two is more relevant for a given data set: if it is important that the really high or really low values are reflected in the measure of centrality, then the mean is better. If not, then we should stick with the median. This decision only really matters if the data are skewed; if the data are symmetric, then the mean and median are going to be approximately equal, and the distinction between them is irrelevant. If the data are skewed, the mean gets pulled in the direction of the skew (i.e., if the data are right-skewed, then the mean will be bigger than the median; if the data are left-skewed, the opposite relation is true).

Learning Objectives

After completing this module, you should be able to:

Measures of centrality like the mean can give us only part of the picture that a data set paints. For example, let’s say you’ve just gotten the results of a standardized test back, and your score was 138. The mean score on the test is 120. So, your score is above average! But how good is it really? If all the scores were between 100 and 140, then you know your score must be among the best. But if the scores ranged from 0 to 200, then maybe 140 is just good-but-not-great (though it is still above average). Knowing information about how the data are spread out can help us put a particular data value in better context. In this module, we’ll look at two numbers that help us describe the spread in the data: the range and the standard deviation. These numbers are called measures of dispersion.

The Range

Our first measure of dispersion is the range. It’s the measure we used in the standardized test example above.

The range of a data set is the difference between the maximum and minimum values in the set.

The Standard Deviation

The standard deviation is a measure of dispersion that can be interpreted as approximately the average distance of every data value from the mean (this distance from the mean is the “deviation” in “standard deviation”).

The standard deviation of a set of data is given by this formula: s=∑(x-x¯)2n-1s=∑(x-x¯)2n-1 Here, xx represents each data value, x¯x¯ is the mean of the data values nn is the number of data values, and the capital sigma (∑∑) indicates that we take a sum. Here’s how we actually implement that formula:
  1. Compute the mean.
  2. Subtract the mean from each data value.
  3. Square those differences.
  4. Add up the results in step 3.
  5. Divide the result in step 4 by n-1n-1.
  6. Take the square root of the result in step 5.
