Introduction

Hypertension, a common health condition, increases a person’s risk for myocardial infarction (heart attack), heart failure, renal disease, and stroke. Antihypertensive drugs are used to treat hypertension. Angina is chest pain caused by reduced blood flow to the heart. Antianginal drugs such as nitrates and calcium channel blockers are commonly used to treat angina. This chapter will explore hypertension and angina, and the pharmacologic and nonpharmacologic treatments for these disease processes.

Hypertension

As The cardiovascular system transports blood and oxygen to needed organs and systems within the body (see Figure 1.1). The blood flow moves from a system of higher resistance to one of lower resistance—from arteries to capillaries to veins. Blood pressure (see Figure 1.2) represents the force that blood flow exerts on arterial walls.

The two elements that determine blood pressure are cardiac output and peripheral vascular resistance. Cardiac output is the product of the heart rate, the number of times the heart beats per minute, and stroke volume, or the amount of blood ejected with each heartbeat (CO=HR×SVCO=HR×SV). This represents the systolic blood pressure reading, the higher pressure (resistance) within the arteries and vessels. Peripheral vascular resistance (also known as systemic vascular resistance) is determined by blood flow and the level of constriction or dilatation within the arteries and vessels. This indicates the diastolic blood pressure (DBP) reading, which is the lower pressure within the arteries and vessels. Blood pressure (BP) is measured in millimeters of mercury (mm Hg) and is expressed as the systolic pressure over the diastolic pressure (for instance, 125/90 mm Hg). Hypertension is when an individual’s blood pressure is above normal limits for a sustained period of time.

Hypertension can be either primary (essential) hypertension or secondary hypertension. Primary hypertension is caused by unknown elements; however, genetics and behavioral and environmental factors have been indicated. These factors include age, high salt intake, insulin resistance, obesity, high alcohol intake, sedentary lifestyle, smoking, and stress. According to the American Heart Association (2022), primary hypertension makes up approximately 90–95% of hypertension cases.

Secondary hypertension results from another disease processes such as conditions involving the central nervous system, endocrine system, and renal system. Secondary hypertension is treated along with the underlying condition. Secondary hypertension may be cured if the treatment for the underlying condition is successful.

Hormones also impact blood pressure regulation. The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) and vasopressin are the main hormones affecting blood pressure homeostasis. RAAS is a compensatory mechanism the body activates during hypotension (when the blood pressure is low). Renin is released from the kidneys in response to low blood pressure. Renin interacts with angiotensinogen and converts it to angiotensin I. The angiotensin-converting enzyme in the pulmonary blood vessel endothelium is then triggered to produce angiotensin II, which constricts arterioles, increasing peripheral resistance and therefore increasing blood pressure. Angiotensin II also stimulates the adrenal medulla to produce catecholamine, which yields aldosterone. Aldosterone causes the kidneys to retain water and sodium, which results in increased blood volume, cardiac output, and blood pressure.

Vasopressin, which is an antidiuretic hormone that regulates reabsorption of water by the kidneys. It is released by the pituitary gland in response to low blood volume. Vasopressin causes retention of body fluids, vasoconstriction, and increased blood pressure.

Grades of Hypertension

The American Heart Association (AHA) (2022) states that normal blood pressure is the level at which minimal vascular damage occurs and blood flow is sufficient to support oxygenation.

Normal blood pressure is considered a systolic reading of less than 120 mm Hg and a diastolic reading of less than 80 mm Hg. Elevated blood pressure and hypertension increase the risk of vascular damage and decrease oxygenation. Diagnostics

A physical examination by a health care provider with a sphygmomanometer (blood pressure monitor) will identify an elevated blood pressure. A 12-lead ECG (electrocardiogram) can assist in determining if the heart has a normal or abnormal rhythm. Blood work is then obtained to determine factors that contribute to hypertension as well as how the body is reacting to elevated blood pressure. These laboratory tests include a complete blood cell count (CBC), basic electrolyte panel, cholesterol panel, and renal function. An echocardiogram (EKG), an ultrasound of the heart, is not typically included in diagnostic procedures to determine hypertension; however, the healthcare provider may add this diagnostic study to visualize the heart’s chambers, valves, and pumping action. Diagnosis for hypertension is based on the findings of the physical examination and the diagnostic study results.

Clinical Manifestations

Symptoms of hypertension may not occur for years, and most of the time the symptoms can be silent. Symptoms typically associated with hypertension include elevated blood pressure readings, headaches, dizziness, nausea, vomiting, visual disturbances, and neurological disturbances such as disorientation or a decreased level of consciousness. Over time, uncontrolled hypertension can lead to organ damage, and clients can experience symptoms related to the specific organ that is damaged; for example, with kidney damage the client may experience end stage renal disease (ESRD) and require dialysis.

Nonpharmacologic Treatment for Hypertension

Nonpharmacologic measures used as treatment for hypertension center around lifestyle changes. Lifestyle changes should focus on the client’s personal, social, and cultural influences. These changes may include diet modification, physical activity and exercise, smoking cessation, alcohol consumption reduction, relaxation techniques, and self-monitoring.

Dietary Modification

Clients with hypertension should eat a low-sodium diet consisting of whole grains, vegetables, and fruits. Other recommendations include low-fat dairy products and limiting meat intake to a total of 6 ounces of lean meat, such as fish and poultry, daily. Reducing sweets and red meat is also important. The American Heart Association (2022) recommends 1500 mg to no more than 2300 mg of sodium intake per day. Dietary pattern and nutritional therapy should focus on food preference, social and cultural influences, and appropriate caloric intake.

Physical Activity and Exercise

Moderate exercise is recommended by the American Heart Association (2022) at least four times a week for adults. Average time for exercise should be at least 30–40 minutes per session to assist in decreasing blood pressure. If the client cannot do 30–40 minutes of moderate exercise four times a week, suggest an alternate exercise plan, emphasizing that some physical activity is better than no physical activity. Physical activity and exercise enhance weight reduction and promote a healthy lifestyle. A weight reduction of at least 10 kg can reduce blood pressure by 5 to 20 mm Hg (Ghadieh, 2015).

Smoking Cessation and Reducing Alcohol Consumption

Tobacco use is the leading preventable cause of coronary artery disease. Smoking increases sympathetic nervous system activity, and therefore increases blood pressure, heart rate, and myocardial contractility. Smoking cessation can decrease the risk of coronary artery disease by more than 30% (Luehrs et al., 2021).

Alcohol consumption increases blood pressure by increasing the level of the hormone renin in the blood. Renin decreases fluid elimination and leads to higher fluid volume in the body, which therefore increases blood pressure. A reduction of alcohol consumption has been shown to decrease both systolic and/or diastolic blood pressure by 5 to 12 mm Hg (Wake, 2021).

Relaxation Techniques

Relaxation techniques can include mindfulness-based stress reduction, transcendental meditation, deep breathing exercises, stretching, tai chi, yoga, and acupuncture. These techniques have been shown to decrease autonomic nervous system activity and lower stress and blood pressure (Brook et al., 2013).

Home blood pressure monitoring (Figure 1.3) is essential in lowering blood pressure. Clients are now able to monitor their blood pressure with electronic blood pressure devices. Home blood pressure monitoring leads to awareness of one’s blood pressure, better blood pressure control, and adherence to lifestyle changes and drug therapies (Verma et al., 2021).

Pharmacologic Treatment of Hypertension

Pharmacologic treatment for hypertension centers on the individual client and takes into consideration their lifestyle as well as their personal, social, and cultural preferences. The objectives of pharmacological treatment are to reduce blood pressure and reduce the long-term effects of hypertension on the body, such as organ damage. The primary antihypertensive drugs used to treat high blood pressure include angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, angiotensin II receptor blockers, beta-adrenergic blockers, calcium channel blockers, and diuretics. These drugs will be discussed further in this chapter.

Clinical Tip

Assessing Therapeutic Effects

Monitor the client’s blood pressure to assess for drug efficacy. If the blood pressure remains elevated despite antihypertensive treatment, the healthcare provider may adjust antihypertensive drugs to better control the client’s blood pressure.

Angina

Angina (also called angina pectoris) is defined as discomfort in the front of the chest, neck, shoulders, jaw, or arms that is precipitated by physical exertions and relieved by rest or sublingual nitrates (Timmis & Roobottom, 2015). Angina is caused by reduced blood flow to the heart. There are four types of angina (Mayo, 2022):

  • Stable angina is the most common form. It occurs during activity and goes away with rest. Stable angina is predictable and follows a pattern similar to the previous episodes of chest pain.
  • Unstable angina is unpredictable and considered a medical emergency. Unlike stable angina, it does not follow a pattern similar to previous episodes of chest pain, and episodes last longer than stable angina episodes. If blood flow to the heart doesn’t improve, a myocardial infarction (heart attack) may occur.
  • Variant (Prinzmetal) angina is caused by a spasm in the hearts arteries and can occur at rest. This type of angina often occurs in cycles, and may occur at rest or overnight.
  • Refractory angina is frequent episodes of angina that occur despite lifestyle changes and medication.

Risk factors for angina include age, family history, tobacco use, diabetes, hypertension, high cholesterol, obesity, emotional stress, and drug misuse (Mayo, 2022).

Pharmacologic Treatment of Angina

Pharmacologic treatment for angina is individualized. Client lifestyle as well as social and cultural influences impact the health care provider’s choice of drug. The goal for pharmacologic treatment of stable angina is to prevent an ischemic event, such as myocardial ischemia (heart attack), from occurring. Nitrates are the first line of treatment for angina, along with beta-adrenergic blockers and calcium channel blockers (Rousan & Thadani, 2019). These drugs will be discussed further in this chapter.

Client Teaching Guidelines

Antihypertensive/Antianginal Use

The client should:

  • Take antihypertensive and antianginal drugs as prescribed by their health care provider.
  • Take their blood pressure and pulse prior to starting an antihypertensive drug, to obtain a baseline reading.
  • Monitor their blood pressure and pulse regularly or as directed by their health care provider and keep a record of the reading for the healthcare provider. The client should report significant changes to their health care provider.
  • Keep a record of the number and severity of angina episodes to review with their healthcare provider.
  • Keep family and support system informed of emergency use of antianginal drugs and their location.
  • Wear a medical alert bracelet and carry a list of drugs they are currently taking.
  • Avoid alcohol, caffeine, and tobacco because these may interfere with the action of antihypertensive and antianginal drugs.

The client should not:

  • Take a double dose of the antihypertensive drug. If the client misses a dose of their antihypertensive drug, they should take it as soon as they remember—unless it’s almost time for the next dose. In that case, they should wait and take the next dose at the normal time.
  • Abruptly discontinue an antihypertensive drug without consulting with the health care provider, because with some drugs this may cause rebound elevated blood pressure and an elevated pulse rate.
  • Take over the counter (OTC) drugs and/or herbal supplements without consulting with their health care provider or pharmacist.

Other concerns:

  • Headaches may occur with the use of nitrates. Acetaminophen may help in relieving these headaches.
  • Antihypertensive drugs may cause orthostatic hypotension (a form of low blood pressure that occurs when going from a sitting or lying position to a standing position). The client should change positions slowly to prevent dizziness and fainting.
  • If low blood pressure develops (feeling dizzy or lightheaded, having blurred vision, feeling weak, or fainting), the client should lie on their back with legs elevated and notify their health care provider.
  • If the client is unsure about how to take their antihypertensive drug, they should call their health care provider or pharmacist.

Introduction and Use

Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors are a classification of drugs that block the body’s production of angiotensin II. Angiotensin II causes vasoconstriction and inhibits the reuptake of norepinephrine, which stimulates catecholamine release. The release of catecholamine decreases urinary excretion of sodium and water, which then causes the release of aldosterone. Aldosterone stimulates the hypertrophy of the vascular smooth muscles, causing thickening of the vascular smooth muscle wall, thereby restricting blood flow. ACE inhibitors (see Figure 1.4 are used to treat hypertension and cardiovascular diseases. They are the first line of treatment for clients who have hypertension and a comorbidity of diabetes.

Special Considerations

ACE Inhibitors

African Americans and clients of African descent with hypertension demonstrate a lower response to ACE inhibitor monotherapy. ACE inhibitors interfere with the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system, which causes high blood pressure when renin levels are high. Clients of African Americans and African descent often have hypertension, but also have lower levels of renin. Adverse Effects

ACE inhibitors are relatively safe to use and have a low incidence of serious adverse effects. Adverse effects include angioedema, non-productive cough, neutropenia (low neutrophils in the blood), agranulocytosis (low granulocytes in the blood), proteinuria (protein in the urine), and rash. Hypotension may develop. Clients may develop an ACE inhibitor–associated cough (a persistent, dry, itchy cough). Hyperkalemia (elevated potassium level in the blood) may develop in clients with diabetes.

Contraindications

ACE inhibitors are a Pregnancy Category D drug and should not be taken during pregnancy. Clients with renal impairment should use cautiously. Clients with a previous hypersensitivity reaction to an ACE inhibitor should not be prescribed this classification of drug.

Nursing Implications

The nurse should assess the client’s blood pressure and pulse on an ongoing basis with initial dosing and intermittently during drug therapy. The nurse also should assess and monitor the client for adverse effects, drug and food interactions, and contraindications. The nurse should provide client teaching regarding the drug and when to call the healthcare provider. See below for additional client teaching guidelines.

Safety Alert

ACE Inhibitors

Taking ACE inhibitors with potassium-containing salt substitutes or consuming large amounts of high potassium foods increases the risk of hyperkalemia. Having a blood potassium level above 6.0 mmol/L is considered a medical emergency and can result in cardiac arrest.

The client should:

  • Avoid foods high in potassium and salt substitutes (because these are high in potassium) due to the aldosterone release.

  • Report symptoms such as chest pain, slurred speech, hemiparesis, and difficulty speaking or walking to their health care provider immediately because these may be symptoms of a heart attack or stroke.

Other concerns:

  • A persistent cough may develop when taking ACE inhibitors.

  • 1.4.2 Differentiate between beta-adrenergic blocker drugs used to treat hypertension.
  • 1.4.3 Describe nursing implications and client education related to beta-adrenergic blocker drugs used to treat hypertension.

Introduction and Use

Beta-adrenergic blockers (beta blockers) are a classification of drugs that inhibit chronotropic, inotropic, and vasoconstrictor response to catecholamine, epinephrine, and norepinephrine by exerting effects on adrenergic receptors beta 1, beta 2, and alpha.

Beta 1 receptors are found primarily in the heart and kidneys, and they increase the heart rate, myocardial activity, and release of renin. Beta 2 receptors are found in the smooth muscle tissue of the heart, lungs, and nervous system. They increase myocardial contractility and cause muscle tremors. Alpha receptors stimulate vasoconstriction.

Beta blockers are classified as either non-selective or cardio-selective. Non-selective beta blockers affect both beta 1 and beta 2 and act on the cardiovascular and respiratory systems. Cardio-selective beta blockers, in contrast, affect alpha 1 and beta 1, which impact the cardiovascular system.

Beta blockers decrease heart rate, decrease myocardial contractility, and decrease the rate of conduction through the AV node, thereby lowering blood pressure. Beta blockers also cause vasodilation and decrease the release of renin and angiotensin II, promoting excretion of sodium and water from the body.

Beta blockers are relatively safe for use. Beta blockers are used to treat clients with hypertension, heart failure, arrhythmias, myocardial infarctions, migraines, glaucoma, and certain types of tremors. Beta blockers have also been used by health care providers as anxiolytics (to reduce anxiety).

Most beta blockers are given orally. Atenolol, labetalol, metoprolol, and propranolol can be administered intravenously. Extended-release beta blockers should not be crushed. Blood pressure and pulse rate should be monitored prior to administration. Beta blockers should not be administered if the client is hypotensive or has a heart rate less than 60.

Special Considerations

Beta Blockers

Beta blockers pose an ethical dilemma for health care providers because they cause anxiolytic effects. The FDA, however, does not support this use for beta blockers. The health care provider may prescribe beta blockers off-label (against FDA-approved labeling indications for drug use) for anxiety. Therefore, the prescribed use of beta blockers for anxiolytics should be weighed against standards of practice as well as risks and benefits to the client.

Beta blockers may mask low blood sugar levels in diabetic clients due to sympathetic nervous system inhibition. Asthmatic clients and clients with chronic lung diseases should be monitored carefully while taking beta blockers due to a decrease in lung function due to beta 2 inhibition. Introduction and Use

Calcium channel blockers are a classification of drugs that block calcium from entering cells by binding to long-acting voltage-gated calcium channels in the heart, smooth muscle, and pancreas. Calcium causes vasoconstriction. Calcium channel blockers inhibit calcium and allow for vasodilation, thereby causing the heart rate to slow and blood pressure to lower. Calcium channel blockers are commonly used to treat hypertension, angina, and arrhythmias.

Calcium channel blockers are classified as nondihydro-pyridines or dihydro-pyridines. Dihydro-pyridines are peripheral vasodilators and lower blood pressure and heart rate; they are used to treat post-intracranial hemorrhage, vasospasm, and migraines. In addition to their action on the peripheral vasculature, nondihydro-pyridines inhibit sinoatrial (SA) and atrioventricular (AV) nodes, slowing cardiac conduction, slowing the heart rate, and decreasing oxygen demand.

Calcium channel blockers may be used alone or in combination with other drugs. They are highly protein bound, which increases the volume of distribution within the body. Calcium channel blockers are available in long- and short-acting formulas.

Special Considerations

Calcium Channel Blockers

Calcium channel blockers are among the most effective antihypertensive classification of drug for use in African Americans, clients of African descent, and older clients.

Thiazide and Thiazide-Like Diuretics

Thiazide and thiazide-like diuretics inhibit the reabsorption of sodium and chloride in the distal renal tubules. They increase the excretion of sodium and water by the kidneys, producing diuresis in the client. Thiazide diuretics are effective, but are not as potent as loop diuretics. They create a potassium loss within the body. Thiazide and thiazide-like diuretics are often the first line of treatment for clients diagnosed with hypertension and may be used in conjunction with other antihypertensive drugs.

Potassium-sparing diuretics antagonize aldosterone. These drugs reduce aldosterone-induced sodium and water retention in the late distal tubules of the kidneys. These types of diuretics spare potassium within the body; therefore, potassium does not need to be supplemented. Potassium-sparing diuretics are commonly used to treat hypertension in clients with impaired renal function.

Clinical Tip

Monitor for Orthostatic Hypotension

When administering nitrates to clients with angina or hypertension, the nurse should monitor the client’s blood pressure for orthostatic hypotension. The client should be instructed to slowly move from a lying to sitting position and then from a sitting to standing position. The client should be monitored closely to prevent falls and injuries.

Rounding Rules

Nurses must know when and how to round. Caplets and tablets can be rounded to the nearest half tablet if they are scored. Some are scored in fourths and can be rounded accordingly. Caplets and tablets that are controlled release, extended release, sustained release, or are enteric coated should not be split or crushed. Capsules are rounded to the nearest whole number because these cannot be divided. Liquid drugs are rounded to the nearest tenth. If you are calculating a liquid medication for drops, then it is to be rounded to the nearest whole number because you cannot administer a partial drop.

Next, rounding is determined by how many places from the decimal point are appropriate. If a number to the right of the decimal point needs to be rounded and it is 4 or less, then it is rounded down. For example, if the number is 1.231 and you are rounding to the nearest tenth, the number would be rounded 1.2. If a number to the right of the decimal point is 5 or greater, then it should be rounded up. For example, if the number is 1.251 and you are rounding to the nearest tenth, the number would be rounded to 1.3.

Cory’s work as a community health nurse reflects the experiences of many nurses today. The world is quickly becoming more global, with an unprecedented spread of people, information, and ideas. One effect of rapid globalization is that the United States population is always changing. Today, more than one in five adults living in the US speaks a language other than English at home (United States Census Bureau, 2022). Each new generation is increasingly diverse, comprised of individuals who identify with a multitude of ethnicities, races, religions, genders, and sexual orientations. This diversity has increased more quickly than society’s ability to navigate the many cultural factors underlying these rapid changes.

Culture, as defined by nurse anthropologist Larry Purnell, is “the totality of socially transmitted behavioral patterns, arts, beliefs, customs, lifeways, and all other products of human work and thought characteristics of a population of people that guide their worldview and decision-making” (Purnell, 2013). Culture is a learned behavior that develops through social and religious structures, intellectual and artistic expressions, and the influence of internal and external stimuli. Culture is characterized by shared values, beliefs, norms, and practices that shape the way individuals think, act, and exist. It is a dynamic and ever-changing process that influences people’s actions and decision making, and helps to shape their identities, roles, and lifeways (Giger & Davidhizar, 2002). Everyone, regardless of where they are born or live, belongs to a culture, or even multiple cultures. Culture is passed down from one generation to the next, shapes the way individuals perceive and respond to the world around them, and guides their beliefs and behaviors. It is important to understand that culture continually changes and shifts; generated by human behavior, culture is a dynamic reflection of interactions and reactions. Because it encompasses and influences so much of individuals’ lives, culture also affects their health as individuals and as populations. Individual responses to cultural processes also vary; while cultures are comprised of many individuals, each one has their own experiences that influence their responses and behaviors.

This chapter provides an overview of cultural competence in health care, exploring the key concepts and frameworks that comprise what it means to be culturally competent. Population health emphasizes equitable access and quality of care regardless of a person’s background. In order to ensure equal access and quality of care for all clients, it is imperative that nurses learn to work across perceived, and real, differences. Nurses work with a variety of populations in a variety of settings, and the ability to provide quality care for all depends on developing cultural competence, beginning with an awareness of their own attitudes, values, and beliefs.

What Is Cultural and Linguistic Competence?

3.1 What Is Cultural and Linguistic Competence?

Learning Outcomes

Like culture, cultural competence is a dynamic process. Although there are many definitions of cultural competence, this chapter will utilize the one adopted by the American Association of Colleges of Nursing (AACN): “cultural competence encompasses the attitudes, knowledge, and skills necessary for providing quality care to diverse populations” (AACN, 2008). The process of developing cultural competence involves individuals and organizations working to understand, respect, and value a person’s or group’s unique background, identity, and characteristics. Greater cultural competence improves a nurse’s ability to understand, communicate with, and effectively provide care to individuals from diverse cultural backgrounds.

Cultural competence does not mean knowing everything about every culture. With hundreds, perhaps thousands, of cultures around the world, all of them continually changing, such knowledge would be impossible. Instead, cultural competence occurs along a continuum, beginning with the first exploration of one’s own attitudes, beliefs, and values and continuing through a reflective process with the aim of providing culturally congruent care, or care that aligns with a client’s or population’s health beliefs and behaviors.

Like cultural competence, linguistic competence also exists along a continuum, and, like cultural competence, it is not based on comprehensive knowledge. Rather, it consists of acquiring the knowledge, attitudes, and skills that enable nurses to care and advocate for clients who communicate in a language other than the one most commonly spoken and/or understood in the service setting.

The Value of Culturally Competent Nursing

The latest research in nursing emphasizes the importance of improving cultural competence to provide culturally congruent care (Im & Chee, 2021). Cultural congruence involves recognizing and respecting cultural preferences and needs, as well as being able to provide optimal care tailored to an individual or population. Recognizing the ways that culture can influence client beliefs and behaviors, as well as the ways in which an individual’s cultural background affects their health care experiences, enables nurses to improve client outcomes, increase client satisfaction, and reduce health inequities. Studies have shown that when nurses are culturally competent, they demonstrate more empathy, communicate more effectively, and provide better client education (Umanzor & Arcaute-Gonzalex, 2019). These skills improve client outcomes, increase satisfaction, and reduce health inequities, which are differences in health resources or health status based on social factors. In addition, nurses who strive to become culturally competent can more readily navigate challenging situations when providing care for diverse populations. They are more likely to be aware of the potential biases that can come into play when delivering care and better able to take steps to minimize those biases. Building relationships of trust between communities and health care professionals can help ensure that individuals receive the appropriate care they need, regardless of their cultural origins.

Culturally Congruent Care

When nurses practice cultural competence, they aim to provide culturally congruent care. Culturally congruent care is consistent with the cultural beliefs, values, and practices of the client and tailored to their cultural needs. For example, a nurse wishes to provide culturally congruent care for a client with diabetes who has stated that they frequently consult with a traditional healer about their dietary choices. To provide culturally congruent care, the nurse takes the client’s beliefs and practices into account, perhaps consulting with a traditional practitioner to incorporate culturally appropriate foods into the client's care plan. The nurse can the client with education about their dietary choices and encourage them to continue incorporating healthy practices that are congruent with their culture.

Reducing Health Inequities

Cultural competence reduces health inequities by increasing awareness and understanding of the cultural beliefs, values, and practices of diverse populations. Cultural competence promotes respect and understanding between nurses and clients, which can bridge differences in cultural backgrounds. Nurses who respect and understand the cultural beliefs and values of a client are better prepared to provide care that is consistent with the client's cultural expectations and practices. This can help to reduce health inequities, as the client is more likely to collaborate with health care providers and to understand and follow the recommended care plan, regardless of their background or circumstances.

Cultural competence reduces barriers and increases access to care. For example, a nurse who coordinates translation for clients who speak another language helps those clients access needed services and health-related information (Foronda & Baptiste, 2022). When practicing cultural competence, nurses provide care that is tailored to the cultural needs of clients, increasing access for underserved and marginalized populations.

The Educational Roots of Health Inequities

The educational roots of health inequities result from institutional and systemic biases in policies that separate individuals into either resource-rich or resource-poor neighborhoods as these neighborhoods dictate school access. This separation into resource-rich or poor neighborhoods is principally based on socioeconomic position and race resulting in wide health disparities in life expectancy and health outcomes as education is a major factor in shaping the course of an individual’s life.

Cultural competence also informs nurses about the social determinants of health (SDOH), which are the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age (Figure 3.2). As discussed in Social Determinants of Health, these conditions include factors such as income, education, housing, employment, and access to health care, and they can create significant health inequities (Office of Disease Prevention and Health Promotion [ODPHP], n.d.). For example, some communities with high levels of poverty are considered “food deserts,” areas where access to affordable and nutritious food is limited or nonexistent. This can be due to a lack of grocery stores in the area, inadequate transportation options, and low-income levels that make it difficult for residents to afford healthy food. Food deserts are a serious public health concern because they can contribute to a number of health problems, including obesity, diabetes, and heart disease. People living in food deserts are often forced to rely on convenience stores and fast-food restaurants, which tend to offer unhealthy food options that are high in calories, fat, and sodium, resulting in a higher prevalence of chronic diseases. A nurse who is aware of the impact of a food desert on the local community can advocate for the increased availability of nutritious foods in places such as corner stores, promote the incorporation of healthy foods into school meals, and provide education to help people find healthy food and make healthier choices.

Improving Health Outcomes

Culturally competent nurses better understand and respond to their clients’ needs. They recognize and respect cultural differences, remain cognizant of personal and structural biases, and provide culturally sensitive care. By understanding the unique cultural needs of their clients, nurses can provide more effective and personalized care, which can lead to improved health outcomes.

As nurses develop their cultural competence, they attain a better understanding of the wide variety of cultures, values, beliefs, practices, and health care needs among different groups. Cultural competence supports the delivery of culturally responsive care, the process of providing care to individuals or populations that fully takes into consideration their cultural backgrounds, values, and preferences. This type of care goes beyond being sensitive to culture; it involves actively ensuring that all aspects of health care are tailored to address the needs and beliefs of a specific population (Hemmings et al., 2016). By providing care consistent with a client’s or group’s specific cultural needs and expectations, nurses foster an understanding and respectful environment, promoting trust and client collaboration. Cultural and linguistic competence also facilitates better communication between nurses and clients, improved health management, increased adherence to treatment plans, and increased client satisfaction. In turn, clients are encouraged to engage with their care, leading to improved health outcomes.

Attitudes, Skills, and Knowledge

The AACN definition of cultural competence is, above all, a call to action: It sets nurses the task of building the attitudes, skills, and knowledge necessary to provide quality care. What are those attitudes, skills, and knowledge?

An open, non-judgmental attitude is essential to providing culturally competent care. A culturally competent nurse displays interest in learning about other beliefs and practices, a willingness to engage in dialogue that encourages learning and mutual respect, motivation to challenge assumptions and biases, a commitment to equity and inclusion, and a desire to help create a safe space for everyone. Culturally competent attitudes also include a positive approach to diversity, valuing and respecting cultural differences, and not discriminating against individuals on the basis of culture.

An attitude critical to cultural competence is cultural humility, which entails acknowledging, respecting, and valuing cultural differences by taking responsibility for one’s own perceptions and ideas. When an individual has cultural humility, they have a deep respect for all cultures as well a desire to learn more and to understand different people’s perspectives. This approach emphasizes self-reflection, open communication, and collaboration across diverse populations. Having humility, or being humble about one’s beliefs and understanding that they are not the best or only way, also involves being aware of one’s own biases and limitations, and understanding that these can influence how one interacts with people from different cultural backgrounds.

Among the many skills required to provide culturally competent care, a few key skills should be part of every nurse’s toolkit. Critical thinking skills are needed to identify and analyze policies, structures, and practices that are oppressive or that disadvantage certain individuals or groups. Linguistic competence is necessary so that nurses can communicate effectively with people from different backgrounds, listening empathetically and openly. The ability to adapt to new cultural environments and navigate unfamiliar situations is essential.

As mentioned above, nurses do not need to possess knowledge of every culture they interact with. In the process of developing cultural competence, nurses seek cultural awareness. In nursing, cultural awareness is defined as “the process of understanding and respecting the beliefs, customs, languages, and values of clients from diverse cultures” (Giger & Davidhizar, 2002; Giger & Davidhizar, 2008). It involves recognizing that culture shapes perspectives and experiences and influences the way people express themselves, and it acknowledges that cultural norms may differ from those most often found in conventional health care settings. Cultural awareness also involves becoming knowledgeable about one's own cultural group and identifying how one's own background may influence their perspectives and behaviors, and how this may impact their interactions with people from other cultures. Cultural awareness involves the ability to recognize and appreciate individual differences and treat them with respect. It is rooted in values such as openness, respect, fairness, and mutual understanding.

Valuing and Respecting Diversity

In valuing diversity, the nurse recognizes and appreciates the unique characteristics, backgrounds, experiences, and perspectives of individuals and groups. Individuals and groups may be diverse in numerous ways, including their culture, race, ethnicity, religion, gender, sexual orientation, age, and ability. Valuing diversity involves not only recognizing that everyone is unique, and that this diversity is a valuable aspect of society, but also treating all individuals with respect and without discrimination. It involves being open to learning from, and collaborating with, people from diverse backgrounds. Valuing diversity can foster a more inclusive and welcoming environment, and it can lead to better outcomes for both individuals and organizations.

Valuing and respecting diversity begins with acknowledging and recognizing the unique backgrounds, experiences, and perspectives of clients and colleagues. This can include learning about the wide variations found in cultural health beliefs and practices, engaging with people from different backgrounds and cultures, and treating all clients with respect and compassion. Effective communication is also critical, as is being open-minded and willing to learn from others. Nurses who advocate for diversity and inclusion in the workplace and recognize and challenge any personal biases or stereotypes that may impact their interactions with others can foster a more inclusive and nurturing environment for their clients, colleagues, communities, and organizations.

Understanding Cultural Identity

Cultural identityis the unique combination of beliefs, values, attitudes, behaviors, customs, practices, experiences, and language that make up an individual's sense of belonging to a particular group or culture. A person’s cultural identity encompasses who they are and how they fit into society, as well as their larger context within the human experience. It is an important aspect of a person's self-concept, and it can change throughout a person’s lifetime. It includes the ways in which one’s beliefs, perspectives, traditions, practices, and values are shaped by the culture, or cultures, to which they belong. Cultural identity creates belonging, connecting individuals with a community and to shared experiences and traditions with others who have a similar background. It can be influenced by external factors such as migration, globalization, and colonization.

Identifying Personal Values and Beliefs

An important first step in the cultural competence process is to develop an understanding of one’s own values and beliefs. This begins with an examination of one’s own life experiences, family background, cultural traditions, religious beliefs, health practices, and personal preferences. It is also important to look at how these values have changed over time, and how they influence the way a nurse interacts with others, including clients, nurse colleagues, and other health care professionals. Additionally, nurses need to examine their own biases to ensure that they are not perpetuating any stereotypes or prejudices when providing care. This process will be covered in more detail in the next section.

Numerous factors can affect the process of providing culturally competent care. Personal factors of the nurse, including their value system, beliefs, and customs, as well as the nurse’s knowledge and attitudes about other cultures, play a role. Cultural factors such as language and communication barriers, gendered roles, and health care practices readily impact the interactions between nurses and clients. Nurses with limited experience interacting with clients from different backgrounds may exhibit biases, othering, and stereotyping in their approach to care.

Personal Factors

An individual’s experiences, cultural background, attitudes, age, and educational preparation are all factors that can affect cultural competence. Younger generations are increasingly more diverse (Pew Research Center, 2018), and older individuals may be less familiar with changing cultural dynamics. Education is also an important factor as it can provide individuals with the knowledge, skills, and critical thinking tools to effectively interact with people from diverse backgrounds. Individuals educated in a diverse environment and with some cross-cultural knowledge may be more adept at interactions with people outside their own culture (Sharifi, 2019). Socialization, personality, and prior experiences also play a role, as people who were raised in diverse communities or have experience traveling may feel more comfortable interacting with people from different cultures in a variety of settings, such as in the workplace, in social situations, and in their community.

Biases and Stereotyping

Biases are prejudicial attitudes or preconceptions that act as filters to the way people perceive, interpret, and react to the world. These opinions or beliefs (involving gender, race, age, or other categories) affect decision making in subtle and overt ways. When nurses focus only on certain information while ignoring other relevant data, biases can lead to incorrect assumptions, judgments, and actions. In health care, biases can cause health care workers to draw false conclusions, lead to disparities in diagnoses and treatments, result in unequal access to services and resources, and result in slower response times for individuals belonging to certain groups, such as people of color, those who identify as LGBTQ+, and people belonging to different religions. Finally, biases can lead nurses to ignore their own prejudices, resulting in a lack of cultural sensitivity and respect.

Stereotyping is the act of ascribing certain characteristics or behaviors to a person based on their membership in a particular group. This can involve making assumptions about people based on their perceived race, ethnicity, religion, gender identity, age, socioeconomic status, ability, or sexual orientation. Stereotyping can lead to assumptions and generalizations about individuals and groups that may not be accurate or fair. In health care, stereotyping can have a negative impact if it leads to unequal, inadequate, or inappropriate client care. Stereotyping can also prevent health care providers from effectively communicating with and understanding the needs of their clients, which can negatively impact the quality of care that clients receive. To guard against the harmful effects of stereotyping, nurses must gather and utilize accurate information about their clients as individuals, rather than relying on learned, and potentially damaging, assumptions.

Othering

Othering is the process of excluding, marginalizing, or differentiating individuals or groups based on their perceived differences from the dominant group in a society. Othering is used to separate or exclude individuals who may not fit within social norms or expectations. It often involves ascribing negative characteristics or traits to individuals or groups based on their perceived differences, implying that a person or group are inferior. This can lead to discrimination, feelings of isolation, and social inequality. Othering can take many forms, such as racism, sexism, homophobia, ableism, and other varieties of discrimination. The practice of othering can have harmful effects on individuals and communities, as it can create barriers to social inclusion, equal opportunities, and fair treatment. It can also reinforce negative stereotypes and prejudices and can prevent individuals and groups from being treated with dignity and respect.

Developing Self-awareness

Self-awareness is an important step in developing cultural competence and cultural humility (Foronda et al., 2018). It involves understanding one’s own values and beliefs, how they are influenced by one’s cultural background, and how they influence one’s perceptions and behaviors. To develop self-awareness, nurses can start by reflecting on their experiences, cultural background, biases, and stereotypes, and how these may impact their views of people from different backgrounds.

There are a number of tools and approaches for working toward this self-awareness. One example is the Implicit Association Test (IAT), a psychological tool used to measure bias and the effect that unconscious attitudes and beliefs have on day-to-day decision making. People of any background can use the IAT to identify hidden biases that may affect their objective evaluation and treatment of others. Once biases are identified, nurses can challenge their biases by engaging in open and respectful conversations with individuals from different backgrounds and asking those individuals questions to learn more about their experiences and perspectives.

Participating in organizations that embrace and promote diversity can also help nurses develop self-awareness and cultural competence. Examples of such activities may include attending cultural events, volunteering at health clinics that serve immigrant communities, or attending an interfaith service. By engaging in these activities, nurses can continue to learn and grow in their understanding of working with individuals and groups from diverse cultural backgrounds, building the skills and knowledge necessary to provide compassionate, culturally congruent care to their clients.

The Colorado Education Initiative has developed a Cultural Competence Self-Awareness Checklist that individuals can use to check their progress toward developing cultural competence.

Barriers to Providing Culturally Competenet Care

Nurses face multiple barriers to providing culturally competent care. They often lack knowledge of different cultural norms and values, face language and communication barriers, and resort to stereotyping due to lack of cultural awareness and limited cultural diversity among health care providers. These challenges can lead to misunderstandings and misdiagnoses, as well as poor client outcomes. In addition, ethnocentrism, gendered roles, organizational barriers, and structural inequities present challenges.

Ethnocentrism

Ethnocentrism is the belief that one's own cultural values, traditions, and practices are superior to those of other cultures (Bizumic & Duckitt, 2012). Ethnocentrism involves judging others by the standards of one’s own culture, assuming that one’s own culture is the norm, or standard, against which all others should be measured. Ethnocentrism can lead to a lack of understanding and respect for other cultures and can result in discrimination, bias, and even violence. Nurses need to be aware of their own ethnocentric tendencies, striving to be more culturally aware and culturally sensitive. Practicing cultural humility allows one to recognize the biases created by ethnocentrism, emphasizing the client’s values, rather than those of the nurse or the dominant culture ((Foronda & Baptiste, 2022).

Gendered Roles

Gender roles and expectations can have a major impact on how individuals from various cultures seek and respond to care. Nursing is often viewed as a gendered role because it has traditionally been associated with women. This gendered association can affect the way that the profession is perceived and valued and can impact interactions between nurses and clients. Nurses should recognize that people of different genders may have varying needs and expectations when it comes to health care. For example, individuals from some cultures may require separate rooms for male and female clients or may prefer that a nurse of the same gender as the client provides care. Understanding how gender and gender roles are viewed from different cultural perspectives can help nurses recognize how gender impacts decision-making, health beliefs, and behaviors of clients when it comes to seeking health care.

Organizational Barriers

Barriers to cultural competence also occur within organizations. These barriers can be far-reaching in scope as they extend well beyond the individual. Factors such as staff education, diversity among health care providers, policies and procedures for addressing cultural differences, and access to language services can all impact the ability of nurses to provide culturally congruent care. Organizational leadership plays a key role in designing and implementing policies that support an inclusive workplace and the provision of culturally and linguistically competent services (Office of Minority Health, 2013). Furthermore, since cultural competence is a process, there must be a commitment to updating and maintaining programs and policies that support it.

Structural Inequities

Structural inequities in health care refer to the systemic barriers and disparities that exist in the health care system that prevent certain individuals or groups from receiving high-quality care. These inequities can be caused by a range of factors, including discriminatory policies and practices, unequal access to health care resources, and inadequate representation of certain groups in the health care workforce.

One example of a structural inequity in health care is the unequal distribution of health care resources, such as diagnostic and treatment facilities, across different communities. For example, individuals in rural areas or those living in communities of lower socioeconomic status may have limited access to specialized medical care. This lack of access to care can have a significant impact on population health, as it can result in individuals not receiving the care they need, leading to an increase in preventable illnesses and chronic conditions and disparities in health outcomes, with individuals living in under-served areas often experiencing worse health outcomes than those living in more affluent areas.

Another example of a structural inequity in health care is the lack of representation of certain groups in the health care workforce. For instance, Black and Latinx individuals are underrepresented in the medical nursing professions, which can lead to a lack of cultural competency and culturally congruent care for clients from these backgrounds and a reduction in the quality of care provided.

The process of becoming culturally competent is ongoing and informed by evidence-based practice. This section compares three models of cultural competence that offer a framework for nursing practice and guide the development of nursing interventions and care. Models of Cultural Competence

Nursing theories and models provide a foundation for assessing and delivering care to individuals, groups, and communities. These theories help to guide research and professional development, providing a structure for evaluating care and determining how best to address different health needs. Additionally, they offer a framework for nursing practice, informing the approach nurses take when developing interventions and providing care.

The Giger and Davidhizar Transcultural Assessment Model

Giger and Davidhizar’s Transcultural Assessment Model is a framework used to evaluate a client’s cultural values and perspectives of health and disease behaviors and their impact (Figure 3.3). According to this model, every individual is culturally unique and should be assessed according to six phenomena: communication, space, social organization, time, environmental control, and biological variations. Communication, both verbal and nonverbal, is important for transmitting cultural norms and expectations, conveying meaning, and forming identity. Space refers to the distance between individuals when they interact; different cultural groups have different behaviors surrounding personal distance and physical interaction. Social organization refers to how a cultural group is organized, and includes factors such as family structure, religious values, and role assignments. Time orientation, the perspective and value placed on the past, present, or future, can vary based on culture. For example, some cultures place a greater emphasis on the present moment and may place less value on planning for the future. This can affect the way group members approach health care, such as their willingness to adhere to long-term treatment plans or to obtain preventative care. On the other hand, cultures that have a more future-oriented perspective place greater emphasis on planning and preparing for the future, including taking steps to prevent illness and maintain good health. Environmental control refers to the belief in one's ability to control and plan for factors in the environment. For example, cultures that emphasize external control may foster a belief in fatalism, discouraging the individual from seeking care. Finally, biological variations are differences between individuals in developmental patterns, disease prevalence, and genetic variations.

Figure 3.3 Giger and Davidhizar’s Transcultural Assessment Model assesses each individual according to six phenomena: communication, space, social organization, time, environmental control, and biological variations. (credit: https://cdn.intechopen.com/pdfs/61494.pdf)

Giger and Davidhizar's Transcultural Model can be used to evaluate the way that different cultural groups perceive and approach illness and disease. For example, in some cultures, illness may be viewed as a natural part of life, with more emphasis on the social and spiritual aspects of disease rather than on physiological or biological manifestations. These individuals may prefer to seek care from traditional healers or practitioners instead of, or addition to, Western medical care. On the other hand, American culture places a greater emphasis on biomedical explanations for illness and tends to prioritize Western medical treatments and interventions.

The Purnell Model for Cultural Competence

The Purnell Model for Cultural Competence (Purnell, 2019) employs an ethnographic approach to promote cultural awareness and appreciation in the context of health care (Figure 3.4). It serves as a tool for understanding and analyzing the cultural factors that can impact health care and client outcomes. The Model consists of 12 domains of cultural knowledge, covering a wide range of concepts and skills related to cultural competence in healthcare, including: overview/heritage, communication, family roles and organization, workforce issues, health care practices, high-risk behaviors, physical characteristics, nutrition, pregnancy and childbearing, death rituals, spirituality, and views of health care providers. Each of these domains covers a range of concepts and topics related to cultural differences and their impact on health care. For example, overview/heritage includes concepts related to a client's country of origin, current residence, education, and occupation, while communication covers concepts related to language, dialects, nonverbal communication, and temporality. Death rituals identifies a client’s cultural beliefs and practices related to death, dying, and bereavement. Pregnancy and childbearing practices vary widely around the world; this domain covers cultural beliefs and practices related to fertility, birth control, pregnancy, childbirth, and postpartum care.

Nurses can implement Purnell’s model throughout the nursing process, using their clinical judgment and collaborating with the client to select and emphasize the components of the model that are most relevant to the individual client’s care (Purnell & Fenkl, 2021). For example, a nurse caring for a client from a culture with strict food taboos may need to assess the client's dietary restrictions and offer culturally appropriate meal options. A nurse caring for a Native American client who follows traditional spiritual practices would elicit the client’s views and preferences about incorporating care from a tribal healer and then work to facilitate that collaboration.

Campinha-Bacote Process of Cultural Competence Model

The Campinha-Bacote model emphasizes that cultural competence is not a fixed attribute or trait, but rather a process that health care professionals engage in to provide high-quality, effective care to clients from diverse cultural backgrounds (Campinha-Bacote, 2002). This process consists of five constructs: cultural awareness, cultural knowledge, cultural skill, cultural encounters, and cultural desire (Figure 3.5). Cultural awareness involves examining and exploring one's own cultural background, beliefs, and biases, and then consciously avoiding these biases when interacting with clients from other cultures. Cultural skill is the ability to collect relevant cultural data in a culturally appropriate manner, perform culturally sensitive assessments, and understand how biological and physiological variations can influence the accuracy of this data. Cultural knowledge entails obtaining a thorough educational foundation about the diversity of cultural groups, including an understanding of variations in health- related beliefs and values, disease incidence and prevalence, and treatment efficacy. In Campinha-Bacote’s model, the process of interacting with clients from different cultural backgrounds—in other words, engaging in cultural encounters—is a way to modify or refine one’s existing beliefs about one’s own culture. These encounters can help prevent stereotyping. In cultural encounters, nurses can assess the client's linguistic needs, with the help of a formally trained interpreter, if necessary, to facilitate communication. Using untrained interpreters, such as friends or family members, may not be effective due to their lack of knowledge about medical terminology and disease pathologies. Finally, cultural desire is the motivation to engage in the process of becoming culturally competent and the commitment to providing culturally responsive care. More recently, Campinha-Bacote has refined her model to include cultural competemility, the combination of cultural competence and cultural humility in the delivery of health care services (Campinha-Bacote, 2018). Cultural competemility is viewed as a synergistic process that infuses cultural humility into the model’s five components of cultural competence, encouraging health care providers to be culturally humble throughout the process of cultural competence.

(credit: https://ojin.nursingworld.org/table-of-contents/volume-24-2019/number-2-may-2019/articles-on-previously-published-topics/intersectionality-approach-to-cultural-competemility/)

Since Campinha-Bacote’s model specifically examines the process of cultural competence, it is readily implemented in clinical situations. For example, a nurse might use the model as a guide to design a cultural competence program for hospital staff. The nurse begins by focusing on cultural awareness, providing tools for participants to examine their own cultural backgrounds and recognize their biases and prejudices. Next, the nurse would include content on cultural knowledge, teaching about the impact of health-related beliefs and practices on client outcomes. Cultural skills can be developed through simulations on collecting cultural data and performing culturally based physical assessments. The nurse would engage participants in cultural encounters by using role-plays or case studies. Finally, the nurse works with the staff to foster cultural desire by encouraging a commitment to providing culturally appropriate care.

Tools for Measuring Cultural Competence

Developing accurate and reliable assessment tools to measure cultural competence has proved challenging for nurse scientists. Measurement tools provide nurses with a way to assess their development of cultural competence and confidence when caring for clients from different backgrounds. While there are numerous tools in the literature, not all of them have been tested and validated. A recent review of tools to measure cultural competence found that psychometric properties were published for a limited number (Yadollahi et al., 2020), three of which are presented here.

The Inventory for Assessing the Process of Cultural Competence among Health Care Professionals

The Inventory for Assessing the Process of Cultural Competence among Health Care Professionals (IAPCC-R) is a tool used to assess the cultural competence of health care professionals according to the five constructs of the Campinha-Bacote Model (Camphina-Bacote, 2002). The IAPCC-R consists of 25 items (five items addressing each construct) evaluated using a 4-point Likert scale. Scores on the IAPCC-R range from 25 to 100, with higher scores indicating a higher level of cultural competence. Completion time is approximately 10–15 minutes. According to Campinha-Bacote, a score of 25–49 indicates a level of cultural incompetence, 50–74 indicates a level of cultural awareness, 75–99 indicates a level of cultural competence, and 100 indicates a level of cultural proficiency. The IAPCC-R has been shown to have good content validity, meaning that it measures what it is intended to measure. In addition, the IAPCC-R has good reliability, meaning that it produces consistent results over time. Dr. Campinha-Bacote’s organization Transcultural C.A.R.E. Associates maintains a website listing known uses of the instrument and includes the reliability and validity if reported (http://transculturalcare.net/iapcc-r/). The IAPCC-R has been widely used in health care research and has been translated into multiple languages, including Swedish, Hebrew, German, Spanish, Korean, Finnish, French, and Japanese. However, one criticism of the tool is its advanced reading level, which may make it more difficult to use.

The Transcultural Self-Efficacy Tool

The Transcultural Self-Efficacy Tool (TSET) is a scale designed to measure the confidence of nurses and nursing students in providing transcultural nursing care (Jeffreys & Dogan, 2010). It consists of 83 items in three categories: cognitive, practical, and affective. The cognitive subscale assesses self-efficacy in terms of knowledge about caring for clients from different cultural backgrounds; the practical subscale evaluates self-efficacy in interactions with individuals from different cultures; and the affective subscale assesses self-efficacy in terms of cultural awareness and acceptance and respect for other cultures. The TSET is answered on a Likert-type scale ranging from 1 (not confident) to 10 (fully confident). The TSET has been found to have good content and construct validity and reliability in multiple studies with nursing students from different cultural backgrounds.

The Cultural Awareness Scale

The Cultural Awareness Scale (CAS), developed by Rew et al. (2003), based on the Purnell Model of Cultural Competence, is designed to measure cultural awareness in nursing students. It consists of 36 items with responses measured on a seven-point Likert-type scale, with responses ranging from "strongly disagree" to "strongly agree." There is also an additional response option of "does not apply." The CAS has five subscales: general education experience, cognitive awareness of attitudes, classroom and clinical instruction, research issues, and clinical practice. It has been tested and translated into multiple languages, including Swedish, Korean, and Turkish. The CAS has been found to have good content validity, internal consistency, and reliability.

Cultural factors play a role in how people communicate and interpret the communication styles of others. Communication is linked to cultural identity, and verbal, nonverbal, and written aspects of communication vary across cultures. When nurses are mindful of and endeavor to understand and respect client cues signaling their communication preferences, they can adapt their communication to meet the needs and preferences of each client. Linguistically competent care begins with assessment; the first step is to identify the client’s primary language, either by asking the client, a family member, or other individual who is familiar with the individual’s language. If the client or group does not speak the same language as the health care provider, it is necessary to use a professional interpreter to facilitate communication (see Working with Translated Materials and Interpreters). When communicating with clients with limited English proficiency, use clear and simple language. Avoid medical jargon or technical terms and allow the client ample time to express themselves and to ask questions.

The Office of Minority Health developed a website that provides information, resources, and continuing education opportunities for health care professionals to learn about culturally and linguistically appropriate services (CLAS). CLAS emphasizes the importance of tailoring health care services to an individual's culture and language preferences, respecting and responding to the health needs and preferences of all individuals to achieve health equity, and ensuring that everyone has access to high quality care.

Differences in Communication Styles

Cultural variations in communication can have a significant impact on the way health care professionals and clients interact with each other. These differences can affect the way individuals understand and interpret messages. Culturally and linguistically competent nurses adapt their communication to align with the client’s preferences to avoid potential misunderstandings and communicate more effectively with clients from different backgrounds.

Tone, volume, and speed of speech vary among cultures. Some cultures may be accustomed to loud, fast, and expressive speech, while others may perceive it as rude or aggressive. Eye contact is an important aspect of communication that can vary across cultures; some clients may consider it rude or disrespectful to make eye contact while speaking to a health care provider, while others may consider it a sign of attentiveness and respect. Culture can play a role in how much facial expressiveness, or the way in which a person's face conveys emotions or reactions, is considered normal or appropriate. In some cultures, low facial expressiveness may be considered typical and may not be interpreted as a lack of understanding or interest. However, in other cultures, a lack of facial expressiveness may be seen as a sign of disinterest or even resistance. Culture can also influence the level of emotional expressiveness that is considered appropriate in social interactions. Some clients may be more open about discussing their feelings and expressing emotions, while others may consider it inappropriate to show strong emotions in public or to discuss their health with people who are not close friends or family.

Cultural differences in communication can impact the way that people understand and interpret messages. For example, in high context cultures, such as those found in Latin American and Asian countries, communication tends to be more subtle, relying more on nonverbal cues and context. Because words alone may not convey the full meaning of a message, it is important to consider the context and relationships between people when communicating. In contrast, low context cultures, such as those found in North America and Northern Europe, tend to be more direct in their communication, using clear and explicit language. The level of directness that is considered appropriate can also vary across cultures. In some cultures, such as those in North America and Northern Europe, direct communication is generally accepted, while in other cultures, such as those in Asia and Latin America, it may be considered rude or disrespectful to be overly direct, particularly when discussing negative or potentially embarrassing topics. Instead, these cultures may use more subtle and implicit language.

In some cultures, communication is more oriented towards the self, while in others it is more focused on others. This difference can be seen in the way people use "I" statements versus third person and plural pronouns. In the United States, the dominant cultural norm is individualistic, meaning that people tend to speak in terms of their own experiences and preferences. In contrast, many other cultural groups are more collectivistic, meaning they prioritize the needs and perspectives of the group or community over the individual. For members of these cultures, it may be more common to speak in the third person and to use plural pronouns rather than "I" statements.

Health Literacy and Low Literacy

Health literacy, or the ability to access, understand, and use health information and services to make informed decisions about one's health, is a critical part of Healthy People 2030. Health literacy is essential to ensure that individuals are empowered to take positive actions to improve their health and well-being. This includes being able to understand and use health-related information and services, such as reading and comprehending medical instructions and labels, following a treatment plan, and communicating effectively with health care providers. This can lead to better health outcomes, such as improved physical and mental health, and it can also help to reduce health care costs by preventing unnecessary hospitalizations and other costly interventions. Additionally, health literacy is important for reducing health disparities and promoting health equity. Research has demonstrated that health literacy can have numerous positive impacts on health care (Shahid et al., 2022). It has been shown to increase the use of preventive health care, reduce unnecessary emergency room visits and preventable hospital stays and readmissions, decrease dosing errors, and assist clients in better managing chronic conditions such as diabetes, high cholesterol, hypertension, and HIV/AIDS. Additionally, health literacy can improve overall health outcomes and enhance all six aims of quality improvement: safety, effectiveness, client-centeredness, timeliness, efficiency, and equity. It can also increase client satisfaction.

Healthy People 2030

One overarching goal of Healthy People 2030 is to remove health disparities and improve health literacy in all people. Health literacy is the ability to access, understand, and use health information and services to make informed decisions about one's health. Healthy People 2030 identifies health literacy as being vital to reaching all Healthy People 2030 objectives. However, health information can be difficult to understand, especially in those with limited proficiency in English. Healthy People 2030 has expanded its definition of health literacy to include not only what individuals can do to improve health literacy, but also to emphasize the role that organizations play in helping individuals find, comprehend, and utilize health information and services so they can participate in decisions about their care. Additionally, this new definition goes beyond simply ensuring that individuals understand health information; it focuses on the individual’s ability to utilize this information to make “well-informed” choices, not just “appropriate” choices.

Low literacy refers to the inability to read, write, and comprehend basic information. It is a widespread issue, with an estimated 15% of adults in the United States having low literacy levels. This can have significant consequences for individuals, as it can limit their ability to access information, learn new skills, and fully participate in society. Low literacy is often linked to other socio-economic factors, such as poverty, low education levels, and limited access to health care. It can also negatively impact health outcomes, as those with low literacy may struggle to understand health information, follow treatment plans, and manage their own health.

Effectively communicating with clients struggling with health literacy or low literacy involves using plain, non-medical language, speaking clearly and slowly, and being specific and concrete. Common words one would use to explain medical information to friends or family, such as using "stomach" or "belly" instead of "abdomen,” are appropriate. It is also important to take note of the words the client uses to describe their illness and to then use those words in the conversation. The use of vague or subjective terms should be avoided, and graphics such as illustrations or 3-D models may facilitate the demonstration of important concepts in a clear and simple manner. Finally, showing a client how to do something, such as exercises or taking medicine, may be clearer than offering the client a verbal explanation.

Developing Culturally Tailored Interventions

Nurse scientists have made significant contributions to the development and implementation of culturally tailored interventions to address the specific needs of different cultural groups and improve their engagement and outcomes. These interventions include modifications to content, language, delivery methods, and other elements to reflect the cultural preferences of target populations. According to Kim et al. (2022), there are eight cultural domains that should be considered when designing culturally tailored interventions. These include: 1) persons, 2) language, 3) contents, 4) context, 5) concepts, 6) metaphors, 7) methods, and 8) goals. These domains refer to the need to consider racial/ethnic differences and similarities within the group, use appropriate language, understand the values and traditions of the targeted cultural group, continuously evaluate contextual changes, align the intervention with the targeted group's belief systems, use culturally relevant symbols and ideas, accurately describe problems and intervention goals, and frame outcomes within the customs and traditions of the targeted group.

While nurse researchers have tailored interventions to be culturally sensitive and relevant for numerous populations, the development of the methodology has been slow; because of this, the science has not kept pace with rapidly shifting demographics and social environments. Furthermore, since culture is a fluid and dynamic process, extensive sub-groups and individual variation often exist within a cultural group. For example, when working with Asian American populations, it is important to recognize that Asian cultures are diverse, with over 50 subgroups within Chinese culture alone. The changing landscape, complexity, and variability make it difficult for current approaches to be effective in many situations, leading to a shift towards personalized interventions that consider multiple cultural and social identities.

Culturally tailored interventions should have flexibility in timing to build trust, identify mutual goals, and effectively complete research. To create a culturally tailored intervention, the nurse first identifies the cultural group on which the intervention will focus. This may be a specific ethnic or racial group, a religious group, a linguistic group, or any other group that has unique customs, experiences, or perspectives. So that the intervention is appropriate, acceptable, and effective, research is needed to understand the beliefs, values, and practices related to the issue being addressed (Im & Chee, 2021). This research may involve leading interviews or focus groups, reading academic literature, and consulting with experts in the field. Based on this research, the intervention is specifically tailored to the particular cultural group. This may involve adapting existing interventions or creating new ones using culturally appropriate materials such as visuals, activities, and language that are respectful of the culture. Members of the cultural group should be involved in the development of the intervention, and their feedback should be sought to ensure that it is tailored to their needs (Kim et al., 2022). Interventions are often tested first with a small group of people from the cultural group to ensure they are effective and meet desired outcomes; this also allows for any necessary adjustments based on feedback and findings. Once an intervention is implemented, the nurse evaluates its effectiveness for the client or population.

Well-designed, evidence-based interventions use relevant cultural examples to facilitate understanding. For example, a culturally tailored intervention utilizing a technology-based program to improve the symptoms of cancer and menopause in Asian American breast cancer survivors used examples that highlighted the cultural value of stoicism towards pain and symptoms, which can often lead Asian American clients to not report their pain and symptoms during their survivorship process (Im et al., 2019).

The US Department of Health & Human Services (USDHHS) has developed one good example of a strategy that can be used to create culturally tailored interventions. The mnemonic CAPABLE, which stands for color, arts, papers, access, buy-in, language, and evaluation, can be used to guide the design of culturally and linguistically appropriate written or visual materials. Different colors may convey different meanings for different groups, religions, cultures, and communities, and it is important to determine whether the colors in the materials have any cultural significance. The nurse should consider whether the images and artwork in the materials reflect the community, and what message they may be sending to the audience. The paper should be easy to handle and read, and the size should be appropriate. The materials should be placed in locations that are easily accessible to all community members and at physical distribution points and height placements that enable easy access. Involving multiple community members in the review process can help create buy-in and awareness. The language used in the materials should be easy to read and understand, appropriate for the community, and written at a reading level that will be understood by the majority of readers. Finally, it is important to evaluate the impact of any changes made to the materials.

Working with the Deaf

Deaf individuals view deafness as a source of cultural membership and pride, rather than as a disability that denies them access to language and communication. Most deaf individuals are born into families with hearing parents and siblings but are exposed to Deaf culture at schools for the deaf or through deaf organizations. In contrast to the hearing community, which tends to define Deaf culture in terms of the loss of hearing ability, Deaf culture defines itself by its use of a unique visual language. There are numerous methods of communicating with a deaf individual without knowing sign language. These include written notes, facial expressions and body language, communication aids, software and technology, sign language apps, or using an interpreter. American Sign Language (ASL) is a visual-gestural language used by most deaf and hard of hearing people in the United States and much of Canada. ASL is a complete language that can express complex ideas, thoughts, and emotions, with its own grammar, vocabulary, and syntax. It has been embraced by the deaf community for two centuries and is an important part of deaf culture.

Working with Translated Materials and Interpreters

Translation and interpretation are two different processes used to exchange information from one language to another. For both, a thorough knowledge of cultural and linguistic differences is necessary to effectively communicate meaning. Translation involves written communication, where written text in one language is converted into written text in another language. Interpretation, on the other hand, involves oral communication, where the words that are spoken in one language are rendered into another spoken language in real time. Because of the difficulties presented by the live setting, interpretation can be more challenging than translation. Some of the challenges interpretation poses can include speakers pausing, using the wrong words, correcting themselves, or using local dialects. Furthermore, unlike translators, interpreters lack the ability to review a text or go back and listen again. This can make it more difficult for interpreters to accurately convey the meaning of the spoken language.

In any clinical setting, it is vital to use a trained and certified interpreter. The interpreter should be treated as a respected professional, and extra time should be allotted for messages to be conveyed and understood. The environment should be free of distractions, and the interpreter should be provided with a summary of the client’s background, goals, and needs for the session. The name of the interpreter should be clearly documented.

When working with a client who needs an interpreter, the nurse should speak directly to the client rather than addressing the interpreter, even if the client makes eye contact with the interpreter. The nurse should observe and monitor non-verbal communication. Clear, simple language without medical jargon should be used, with sentence-by-sentence interpretation to ensure that all important information is conveyed. The interpreter may ask open-ended questions to establish rapport or clarify client statements; during these times, the nurse should avoid interrupting the interpreter. Finally, near the end of a visit, the nurse should use the "teach back" method to confirm that the client understands directions and recommendations.

3.5 Providing Culturally and Linguistically Competent Care

Cultural competence is the process of understanding, respecting, and valuing the unique characteristics of individuals and groups. Linguistic competence is the ability to communicate effectively with individuals from different backgrounds. Culturally competent care and the identification of social determinants of health can reduce health care inequities since care is tailored to meet the unique needs of a group or an individual. Cultural competence requires that the nurse be open and non-judgmental, that they understand their own values and beliefs and take responsibility for their perceptions and ideas, and that they practice cultural humility, a process of recognizing, respecting, and valuing cultural differences.

Biases (prejudicial attitudes or preconceptions that influence the way one perceives, interprets, and reacts to the world) and stereotypes (ascribing certain characteristics or behaviors based on an individual’s belonging to a certain group) can negatively impact cultural competence. Othering occurs when a person excludes, marginalizes, or discriminates based on how an individual or group differs from the dominant group. Ethnocentrism, the belief that one’s own culture is superior to others, can also be a barrier to providing culturally competent care. To overcome these barriers, nurses need to engage in self-awareness of their own values and beliefs.

Many theories have been developed to understand cultural competence. Giger and Davidhizar’s Transcultural Model posits that every individual should be assessed according to six phenomena: communication, space, social organization, time, environmental control, and biological variations, and that these factors affect how the individual perceives illness and disease. The Purnell Model of Cultural Competence analyzes 12 domains that affect health care: information about a client’s country of origin, communication style, family structure and roles, work and career, physical characteristics and health, risk behaviors, nutrition, pregnancy and childbirth practices, death rituals, spirituality, health care beliefs and practices, and views of health care providers. Campinha-Bacote’s Process of Cultural Competence Model examines the process of cultural competence using five constructs: cultural awareness, cultural knowledge, cultural skill, cultural encounters, and cultural desire. Various tools exist to measure cultural competence, including the Inventory for Assessing the Process of Cultural Competence among Healthcare Professionals Revised (IAPCC-R) using the five constructs of Campinha-Bacote’s model, the Transcultural Self-Efficacy Tool (TSET) that measures self-efficacy in various aspects of transcultural care, and the Cultural Awareness Scale, based on the Purnell Model, to measure cultural awareness.

Different communication and linguistic styles affect how individuals understand and interpret messages. Factors that can affect communication between cultures include tone, volume, and speed of speech; eye contact; facial expressions; emotional expressiveness; and high- and low-context communication. Health literacy is the ability to access, understand, and use health information or services to make informed decisions about health. The promotion of health literacy can reduce health disparities and promote health equity.

Culturally tailored interventions address the specific needs of different cultural groups and improve their engagement and outcomes. Such interventions include modifying content, language, delivery methods, and other elements to reflect a target population’s cultural preferences. To design a culturally tailored intervention, the nurse identifies the cultural group, researches the group’s beliefs, values, and practices related to the issue being addressed, tailors the intervention with input from group members, implements the intervention, and finally evaluates its effectiveness. Nurses may use numerous strategies for communicating with deaf individuals, such as ASL, written notes, facial expressions, body language, communication aids, technology, and an interpreter. Strategies for communicating with clients who need an interpreter include speaking directly to the client, observing and monitoring non-verbal communication, using clear, jargon-free language, using sentence-by-sentence interpretation, and using the "teach back" method.

Introduction

As much as eating is an enjoyment and part of everyday life for people, it is an essential part of human survival. Proper nutrition depends on ingestion, or the taking in of substances into the body by swallowing or absorbing, of a balanced mix of foods to function, grow, and heal appropriately. Nutrition does not stop at ingestion; rather, this is only the beginning. Nutrition is a combination of ingestion, digestion (the body process of breaking down solid food through a systemic process that includes both mechanical and chemical actions to form smaller, more absorbable substances in the body’s gastrointestinal tract), absorption (the taking in of the broken-down substance into the cells), and metabolism (the processes the body uses to convert the absorbed substances to energy) of needed nutrients, fluids, and minerals.

4.1 Organs of Digestion

Digestion occurs as an integrated process between the upper and lower gastrointestinal tract and accessory systems in the body. Proper digestive processes are needed to ensure nutrients are supplied to the body and homeostasis through fluid and electrolyte balance, adequate nutritional status, proper growth patterns, and body repair needs are maintained. Common end products of digestion are amino acids, simple carbohydrates, lipids, vitamins, and minerals.

The digestive system is responsible for digestion in the body. The main organs of digestion, the gastrointestinal system, begin at the mouth and end at the anus. This system is divided into the upper gastrointestinal system, which starts at the mouth and ends at the duodenum (the very start of the small intestines), and the lower gastrointestinal system, which starts with the small intestines and includes the large intestines (or colon), rectum, and anus. Other organs in the digestive system that are not along the main gastrointestinal system tract that aid in digestion but do not come in direct contact with food, the accessory organs, include the pancreas, liver, and gallbladder (Figure 4.2).

Figure 4.2 The digestive system consists of the upper and lower gastrointestinal tract and accessory systems. (credit: TBD)

It is the purpose of this system and the accessory systems and organs that work with it to receive food, release nutrients and water from the food through digestive processes, deliver these nutrients into the blood and absorb the water that passes through the colon for body use, and to finally excrete nondigestible solid waste from the body (U. S. National Institutes of Health, National Cancer Institute, n.d.).

Organs of the Upper Gastrointestinal System

The upper gastrointestinal system is comprised of the mouth, esophagus, stomach, and duodenum. Food enters the gastrointestinal tract through the mouth, and the mouth has several components that are important to normal digestive processes. The teeth, tongue, and salivary glands facilitate the breakdown of food through mechanical (chewing) and chemical (enzymes) means as accessory organs in the digestive system. Without any one of these components, the mouth would not be able to fully function as it is designed to in the digestive process.

Food then travels from the mouth, through the upper esophageal sphincter, down the esophagus, through the lower esophageal sphincter, into the stomach, out of the stomach through the pyloric sphincter, and into the duodenum (Figure 4.3). The process of digestion through the upper gastrointestinal tract, which ends in gastric emptying, can take from two to five hours to complete (Readman, 2022).

Organs of the Lower Gastrointestinal System

The lower gastrointestinal system is comprised of the small intestines, large intestines, rectum, and anus Food exits the body as waste through the anus in the lower gastrointestinal system. Food enters this portion of the gastrointestinal system tract through the duodenum after gastric emptying occurs. By this time, food is no longer in its solid state. It has been converted to a substance known as chyme, a mix of gastric juices and digested food. After digestive processes are complete in the duodenum, the chyme moves into and through the small intestines, through the large intestines, down to the rectum, and out of the body through the anus. The process of digestion through the lower gastrointestinal tract can take from 10 to 59 hours (Readman, 2022).

Accessory Organs and Systems Impacting Digestion

Organs that lie outside of the main gastrointestinal tract but are still considered part of the digestion system as they directly aid in digestion through release of enzymes and digestive fluids include the pancreas, liver, and gallbladder (GI Associates and Endoscopy Center, 2022). Other organs, body structures, and systems within the body that directly or indirectly aid in digestion in the body but are not considered a part of the digestion system include teeth, saliva glands, the tongue, the sensory system, the nervous system, mucous membranes, and glands (University of Rochester Medical Center, 2022). These organs and structures are not considered a part of the digestion system because they are either part of another organ or body structure, part of another main body system, or their function towards digestion only serves another body function and is not a primary function. Without them, however, the digestive process would malfunction and not be able to be adequately performed by the main gastrointestinal system in all areas of its expected function.

The accessory organs’ main function is assisting in the chemical digestive process. The pancreas, liver, and gallbladder have this as a primary function related to the digestive system. The pancreas, while it performs other functions for the body, produces amylase, lipase, trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, elastase, carboxypeptidase, nucleases, and protease that help to chemically digest sugars, fats, and starches (GI Associates and Endoscopy Center, 2022; National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases, 2022; U. S. National Institutes of Health, National Cancer Institute, n.d.). The pancreas is also responsible for the production of insulin, which is responsible for assisting in the absorption process of glucose into cells. The liver creates and secretes bile to aid in the chemical breakdown of fats into amino acids. The gallbladder stores and concentrates bile for release into the duodenum. The bile’s release into the duodenum is triggered by the cells in the duodenum with the release of the hormone cholecystokinin (Holman et al., 2019; National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases, 2022; (U. S. National Institutes of Health, National Cancer Institute, n.d.).

The other systems impacting the digestive processes do so through triggering of production for enzymes, release of enzymes, or triggering of certain other body processes that allow for both chemical and mechanical processes for digestion, absorption, or metabolism based on the body’s specific needs. The nervous system in the gastrointestinal tract is a mesh-like system of neurons that coordinates the whole process of secretion and digestion in the intestines and is responsible for peristalsis (Holman et al., 2019). The sensory system aids in the desire to ingest as well as the release of saliva as the sight, smell, and taste of food will trigger saliva to be released. Glands and mucosal membranes of the intestinal walls secrete small amounts of digestive enzymes and lubricating mucous to assist with waste movement and absorb nutrients from the food as it is traveling through (Holman et al., 2019; U. S. National Institutes of Health, National Cancer Institute, n.d.).

Congenital Anomalies within Digestive Organs

While rare, there are many congenital anomalies, or birth defects, that can affect the digestive system’s ability to complete its required functions. The majority of them cause blockages in the exit of waste products and result from undeveloped or incorrectly positioned organs (Columbia University Irving Medical Center, n.d.). These abnormalities can generally be classified as atresia, stenosis, and malformations, malrotation and volvulus, Meckel’s diverticulum, lymphangiectasia, Hirschsprung’s disease, and other (Columbia University Irving Medical Center, n.d.; Ludwig et al., 2022), which are detailed in . Some of these abnormalities are severe enough to be detected in-utero or even very soon after birth, while some remain undetected through childhood until they cause physical symptoms (Ludwig et al., 2022).

Atresia, Stenosis, and Malformation

Atresia is a complete blockage in the system, and stenosis is a partial blockage due to a hardening and narrowing of an area. Malformations are an abnormal formation of a specific body part. This type of congenital anomaly has several different types, including anorectal, biliary, esophageal, gastric, and intestinal.

Assessment of the Organs of Digestion

4.2 Assessment of the Organs of Digestion

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • 4.2.1 Describe the assessment of the organs of digestion.
  • 4.2.2 Describe the expected assessment findings of the organs of digestion.

Assessment of the gastrointestinal system is imperative in both prevention and health maintenance in many diseases and in regard to promotion and maintenance of nutrition. The body relies totally on this system to break down food intake into usable nutrition so proper assessment of this system is extremely important to nutritional health. Identifying and, if possible, rectifying any issues found in the normal function and anatomy of this system will aid the client in staying at optimal nutritional health. While physical assessment of the client is required, the nurse should also obtain a history of the client’s dietary practices, normal eating and bowel patterns, any dietary intolerances or allergies, any changes in normal eating and bowel patterns, recent unintentional weight loss or gain, and any medical diagnoses or surgical history. The nurse will also need to obtain the client’s weight and height so that an accurate body mass index (BMI) can be calculated.

Mouth and Oropharynx Assessment

Assessment of the mouth and oropharynx is an assessment of the majority of the upper gastrointestinal system. The nurse will inspect the mouth and oropharynx, beginning with the outer lips to judge for symmetry, color, texture, and contouring. The client should be asked to purse their lips as if to form a whistle to judge for movement ability and symmetry. Next, the inner lips and buccal mucosa should be inspected for color, texture, moisture, and presence of lesions or other abnormal structures. To best visualize these structures, the nurse should direct the client to relax the mouth. Then the nurse should pull the bottom lip outward and away from the client’s teeth by grasping the lip between the thumb and index finger on each side of the lip and carefully pulling outward (Pearson Education, 2018).

There are expected normal findings for each portion of this assessment. The lips are expected to be symmetrical and uniform in color (and appropriate coloration for race), soft, moist, smooth textured, symmetrically contoured, and able to be pursed easily and symmetrically (Pearson Education, 2018). Drooling may be seen in young infants and toddlers up to age two, but should not be excessive. Deviations from these findings are abnormal and should be reported. Abnormal findings that should be reported immediately include cyanosis, which can indicate low oxygen levels, and sudden and new unilateral asymmetry with movement as this could indicate a cerebrovascular accident.

Deviations from these normal findings that could be indicative of nutritional deficiencies or fluid deficits include fissures, crusts, or scales on the lips or in the corners of the lips. Specifically, Vitamin B1, Thiamine, Vitamin B12, and iron, can all be visibly detected in a client’s outer oral assessment by the presence of cracked lips or angular cheilitis (Hakes, 2021).

Inspection of the teeth and gums should be done while inspecting the buccal mucosa. To accomplish this, the nurse should:

  1. Direct the client to open their mouth.
  2. Use a tongue blade/depressor to retract each cheek while using a flashlight or penlight to properly visualize the surfaces. When evaluating the teeth at the back of the mouth on either side, the nurse should direct the client to close their mouth.
  3. Place the index fingers of both hands into one side of the mouth, ask the client to open their mouth, and retract each cheek using the index fingers.
  4. Direct the client to slowly open and close their mouth while the cheek is retracted so that tooth alignment can be visualized.
  5. Inspect the gums should be inspected in this same manner.

If the client has dentures, bridges, or other removable dental appliances, have them remove them and repeat this evaluation for the gums separately. Check gums for retraction, color, bleeding, edema, or lesions (Pearson Education, 2018).

The buccal mucosa and inner lips are expected to be uniform color that is appropriate for race (or freckled brown in darker-skinned individuals), moist (may be slightly drier in older clients due to decreased salivation), glistening, smooth, and elastic texture (Pearson Education, 2018). Gums should be moist and firm without retraction away from the teeth, however, in older clients, there may be some receding of the gum line and some hyperpigmentation (Holman et al., 2021; Pearson Education, 2018). The tongue should rest in the central position in the mouth but move freely, have no tenderness or swelling, be smooth with lateral margins and raised papillae (taste buds), and free of lesions (Pearson Education, 2018). The base of the tongue should be smooth and prominent with veins and absent of ulceration. The gag reflex should be present. All deviations from these findings are abnormal and should be reported. Abnormal findings that should be reported immediately include cyanosis as this could indicate low oxygen levels, and any suspicious lesions, as these could indicate oral cancers, and any plaques, as these can indicate infection.

There are multiple deviations in these areas of the assessment that can indicate nutritional deficiency. Inflammation of the tongue and ulcerative gingivitis could be indicative of Vitamin B2 (Riboflavin) and Vitamin B2 deficiencies. Loss of taste can be the result of a Zinc deficiency (Stewart, 2022). Vitamin B12 deficiency is indicated in some clients by halitosis, hemorrhagic gingivitis, and painful mouth ulcers. Vitamin C deficiency is also a culprit that causes bleeding gingivitis (Hakes, 2021). Stomatitis, mouth inflammation, can be the result of Vitamin B6, B2 (Riboflavin), and B12 deficiencies (American Dental Association, 2022; Hakes, 2021). Iron deficiency can be indicated by dryness from salivary gland dysfunction and dysphagia and a red, painful tongue that has a burning sensation (Hakes, 2021). Zinc deficiency has been linked to recurrent aphthous ulcers, known as canker sores, in the mouth (American Dental Association, 2022).

Abdominal Assessment

Assessment of the abdomen is an assessment of the rest of the upper gastrointestinal system (stomach and duodenum), the accessory organs of the digestive system (pancreas, liver, and gallbladder), and the majority of the lower gastrointestinal system (large and small intestines). Assessment of the abdomen must occur in a specific order: inspection, auscultation, percussion, and then palpation. Auscultating before percussing and palpating ensures the assessor is listening to undisturbed bowel sounds and leaving palpation for last ensures that the potentially most painful portion of the assessment is completed last so that most of the data is collected before discomfort is caused to the client (Hakes, 2021).

Clinical Tip

Positioning the Client for Abdominal Assessment

When preparing the client for abdominal assessment, it is important to position them so that their abdominal wall musculature is relaxed. To achieve this, have the client lie supine and support the head and behind the knees with pillows.

Inspection

Inspection should be the first step in the assessment of the abdomen. This should be done with the client on a completely supine position with the abdomen exposed (Roscoe, 2022). Inspect the skin integrity, abdomen contour, symmetry, and vascular patterns (Pearson Education, 2018). Visualize abdominal movement associated with respirations, aortic blood flow, and peristalsis.

Auscultation

Auscultation of the abdomen is the next step in the assessment. When auscultating the abdomen, listen for bowel sounds, peritoneal friction rubs, and vascular sounds with a stethoscope. Bowel sounds should be listened for in each of the four quadrants of the abdomen with the diaphragm of the stethoscope calculating the rate after listening for at least two minutes in each quadrant and no longer than five minutes (Mealie et al., 2022; Pearson Education, 2018). The diaphragm of the stethoscope should also be used to listen for the absence of peritoneal friction rubs over the spleen and liver as the client breathes in deeply. The bell of the stethoscope should be used when evaluating for vascular sounds. The bell should be used to listen over the aorta, renal arteries, femoral arteries, and iliac arteries in the abdomen to listen for the absence of bruits (Pearson Education, 2018). If there is suspected delay in gastric emptying, place the stethoscope on the abdomen near the stomach, hold the client’s hips and gently shake them from side to side while listening to their abdomen for a succession splash (Mealie et al., 2022). This may be uncomfortable to the client, so the nurse should inform the client on what to expect.

Percussion

Percussion of the abdomen should be done next to determine the presence of gas, fluid, or masses. Using a systematic pattern beginning in the lower right quadrant proceeding in a pattern towards the upper right quadrant, upper left quadrant, and then lower left quadrant, percuss several areas of each quadrant to listen to the quality of the sound; tympany relates to gas, or flatus, and dullness, decrease, absence, or flatness in sound relates to fluid or solid masses. Percussion is also used to best judge size of the spleen and liver (Mealie et al., 2022). To percuss for splenic size, the most inferior interspace on the left anterior axillary line, or the Castell’s point, should be percussed at the client breathes in deeply to listen for the start of dullness from tympany to discover the splenic edge. On the right side, percuss down from the lung and up from the bowel to discover the change from tympany to dullness to locate the liver margins.

Palpation

The last step in the abdominal assessment is palpation. For superficial palpation, the palm should be held slightly above the abdomen with the fingers parallel and then the abdominal wall is depressed lightly at only about one centimeter with the finger pads, which is about the level of the subcutaneous tissue (Pearson Education, 2018). The finger pads should be moved around in a circular motion over all the quadrants while noting any areas of tenderness, masses, crepitus, irregularity in contour, or muscle guarding. There are expected normal findings in each portion of this assessment. On inspection, the skin is expected to be intact, uniform in color, absent of ecchymosis, coloring consistent with race, absent of jaundice, and silver-white striae (stretch marks) and not purple-pink striae unless actively pregnant, or surgical scars if applicable to that client (Pearson Education, 2018). During auscultation deviations can be found in any of the three main sounds. With vascular sounds, the presence of a bruit in any site is abnormal and could indicate possible aneurysm (Pearson Education, 2018). When listening for peritoneal friction rubs, if any are heard in any auscultated area of the abdomen, this is an abnormal finding and could be indicative of peritoneal inflammation. Bowel sounds that deviate from the normal findings can have different indications depending on what deviation is discovered. All deviations should be reported; however, any symptoms of aneurysm, ileus, obstruction, or peritoneal inflammation should be reported immediately.

During percussion, if there is an unexpected sound in an area or large areas of the same sound, this is considered abnormal. Large areas of dullness can be associated with tumors or fluid collections like ascites (Pearson Education, 2018). Organs like the stomach that is air-filled is expected to sound tympanic; if it is dull or flat, this is abnormal (Mealie et al., 2022). All deviations should be reported immediately as these all can be very serious.

Normal Function of the Digestive Organs

Before food ever enters the body, the sensory system, an accessory system to the gastrointestinal system, assists in the start of the digestive process. The sight and smell of food as well as the feel of food entering the mouth and the taste of food when it first contacts the tongue all influence the desire to eat. These senses also trigger the salivary glands to start the release of saliva in the anticipation of eating, which will help with the breakdown of food once it enters the oral cavity.

Inportant Newborn Assessment

For a newborn, anal patency should be established. This is done by two means: first, the rectal temperature is taken by using a lubricated rectal thermometer and inserting it one to two centimeters to record the newborn’s temperature, and secondly, the passage of their first meconium stool confirms patency (Gantan & Wiedrich, 2022).

When food enters the body, the first organ it will contact is the mouth. Within the mouth, or oral cavity, are the tongue, teeth, and salivary glands. The teeth will cut, bite, chew, and grind food down into smaller particles. The teeth work together seamlessly with the tongue, which aids in the mastication process by use of mechanical compression, abrasion, and distortion (Ogobuiro et al., 2022). As the tongue performs these mechanical processes, it also secretes mucins and lingual lipase to help break down lipids. At the same time, the parotid, sublingual, and submandibular salivary glands release a mixture of buffers, glycoproteins known as mucins, and salivary amylase which assist in lubrication of foods for swallowing and starting the process of lipid and complex carbohydrate and starch digestion (National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases, 2022; Ogobuiro et al., 2022).

The next stop for the food will be in the pharynx. In order to swallow food into the esophagus and not the trachea, the swallowing center in the brain stem of the central nervous system triggers certain actions during swallowing. The nasopharynx is closed off by the rise of the soft palette when the tongue initiates swallowing by pushing food to the back of the mouth and the larynx is closed in the pharynx to prevent food from entering the trachea (Ogobuiro et al., 2022). Once this swallowing reflex is initiating, it cannot be stopped until completed.

While in the stomach, food is temporarily stored while mechanical and chemical processes break it down further into a substance known as chyme. The stomach uses its three muscular layers (the inner oblique, middle circular, and external longitudinal layers) to contract and relax in a mixing and churning action to help mechanically break down food. To assist with the chemical breakdown of food, the stomach secretes stomach acids, specifically hydrochloric acid secreted by the parietal cells in the stomach, and enzymes, such as pepsin, somatostatin, and gastrin (Ogobuiro et al., 2022). The stomach is also responsible for secreting intrinsic factor from the parietal cells to be able to absorb Vitamin B12 from the food as it is being digested.

Pathophysiology Associated with Digestive Organs and Function

Many causes of diseases and disorders that impact the gastrointestinal system are linked with nutrition, as are many of their treatments or controls. The digestive system not only is responsible for ensuring the rest of the body is nourished, it is also responsible for nourishing itself. Diseases of this system can impact any portion of the system, from the mouth to the anus and the accessory organs.

Starting in the oral cavity, the client can experience salivary gland tumors, gingivitis of the gums, periodontal disease, and cancers in the mouth. In infants, they can have malformations such as cleft lip or palate which causes a long fissure in the palate of the mouth and lip. This malformation makes feeding an infant dangerous, and a nasogastric tube is needed until repair can be made. These malformations have been linked to maternal deficiency of vitamin B12 in their diets during pregnancy and can cause nutritional deficiencies in the infant until corrected (Munger et al, 2021).

Diseases in the accessory organs can include cholelithiasis, cholecystitis, cirrhosis, hepatitis, pancreatitis, biliary atresia, Gilbert syndrome, Dublin-Johnson syndrome, Crigler-Najjar syndrome, liver cancer, pancreatic cancer, pancreatic stones, pancreatic pseudocyst, choledocholithiasis, hemochromatosis, and Wilson’s disease (Cleveland Clinic, 2022b). In the rectum and anus, diseases can include anal fissures, anal fistulas, rectal cancer, perianal abscesses, and internal and external hemorrhoids. Infants can suffer from neonatal cholestasis (or a failure of bile to flow) that can result from malformation, biliary cysts that block the biliary tree, or inspissated bile duct syndrome in cystic fibrosis and result in hyperbilirubinemia and jaundice (Cochran, 2022f).

Aging of the Digestive System

There are some specific changes to consider with ageing in regard to the digestive system that impact the function of the system and the overall nutrition status of older adults. Older adults tend to have a decrease in appetite over younger adults. There are multiple reasons for this reduced appetite. Older adults tend to be on multiple medications which can decrease their desire to eat, cause nausea, and restrict their dietary food choices due to drug interactions. Older adults also tend to have more comorbidities, causing more restrictions on their diet and decreasing their desire to eat. Many older adults may become lactose intolerant because the production of the lactase enzyme reduces with age, restricting dietary options (Bartel, 2022; Nigam & Knight, 2017).

As part of normal ageing, the senses of smell and taste can decrease, and often sight can be diminished causing less desire to eat because individuals use these senses to choose what they want to eat and how much (Nigam & Knight, 2017). Also, physically, appetite decreases due to changes in the release of hormones that control appetite (ghrelin, peptide tyrosine [PYY], cholecystokinin [CCK], insulin, and leptin).

As individuals age, increased dry mouth, gum retraction, shrinking of the maxillary and mandibular bones, reduction of calcium reserves that increase tooth decay, slowing of the muscular contractions that initiate swallowing, loss of tension in the upper and lower esophageal sphincters, slowing of esophageal peristalsis, and possible loss of gag reflex can impact the ability to safely swallow, calling for a need to change diet consistency to a pureed or liquid form which affects the perceived attractiveness of food and further decreases appetite (Bartel, 2022; Nigam & Knight, 2017).

Older adults are not able to eat as much because their stomach walls lose elasticity as they age, making it less able to accommodate food. Many older adults will lose some secretion of the hydrochloric acid into the stomach to help digest food, and they have a slower gastric emptying. These changes cause older adults to feel fuller faster and longer, decreasing appetite.

Due to loss of tension in the lower esophageal sphincter, older adults often experience from reflux because stomach contents can backflow easier into the esophagus (Nigam & Knight, 2017). The hyposecretion of hydrochloric acid and protective mucous that occurs with age can result in chronic atrophic gastritis, increased peptic ulcer disease, and decreased Vitamin B12 absorption.

Older adults tend to experience more with bloating and abdominal pain as well as decreased absorption of calcium, folic acid, and iron in the small intestines due to an increase of the bacterial populations that reside in the small intestines that occurs with increased age (Bartel, 2022; Nigam & Knight, 2017). The pancreas and liver further reduce the ability of the small intestines to absorb nutrients because, with age, the pancreas decreases its release of digestive enzymes reducing complete digestion of proteins and fats, and the liver decreases its production of bile to assist with protein and fat digestion, decreases its rate of protein synthesis and metabolism, and decreases in its ability to detoxify substances (Nigam & Knight, 2017). There is also an increased risk of constipation with older adults because, in the large intestines, there are reductions in neurotransmitters and neuroreceptors, atrophies of mucosa and muscle layers, formations of diverticula, declines in the rate of cell division in the digestive epithelium, and increases in inflammation conditions due to the increase in facultative anaerobes. The increase in constipation increases the risk of the development of hemorrhoids.

All the changes make it important that the older client is educated to ensure they are eating nutrient-dense options to ensure they are getting adequate nutritional intake. They are likely to require less caloric intake since their metabolic rate decreases with age, so caloric intake does not need to increase, only the nutritional value of the foods they ingest.

To maintain healthy digestion and absorption of food and in turn nutritional health, the gut must maintain a healthy homeostasis, or equilibrium maintained by the physiological processes and evidenced by stable growth of intestinal microbiota. The nurse plays an important role in the education and care of their clients to ensure they are at their optimal nutritional health.

Gut Health Homeostasis

The homeostasis of the gut is strongly impacted by the composition of the gut’s microbiota or normal microbial growth, and diet can impact the health of this microbiota (Lee et al., 2022). When the gut is not in a state of homeostasis, the imbalance that results and causes disease is known as dysbiosis. Many diseases have been linked to dysbiosis, including anxiety, depression, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, cancer, inflammatory bowel disease, obesity, and diabetes, so the impact of not maintaining gut homeostasis far surpasses only gastrointestinal health (Afzaal et al., 2022).

To maintain proper gut homeostasis, the bacterial growth requires energy in the form of adenosine triphosphate and oxygen (Lee et al., 2022). Dietary factors, antibiotics, and underlying host conditions can reduce oxygen consumption in the colonic epithelium, thereby reducing the available oxygen to the indigenous microbes.

The microbiota of the gut has many functions in the body. These bacterial residents help control nutrient metabolism; defend against pathogens; contribute to carbohydrate digestion; synthesize methane from H2 processed by bacterial metabolism; synthesize biotin, folate and Vitamin K; and neutralize carcinogens (Afzaal et al., 2022).

Diet and Gut Health

To maintain appropriate gut homeostasis, the client must maintain an appropriate diet that will feed the microbiota of their gut appropriately. Diet can negatively impact the microbiota of the gut, causing dysbiosis and resultant disease. High-fat diets have been shown to have a significantly negative impact on the health of the gut and contribute to diabetes, obesity, metabolic syndrome, and colon cancer (Afzaal et al., 2022). High sodium intake and alcohol use have both been attributed to dysbiosis and linked to hypertension. Dietary items that have been shown to be helpful to the microbiota of the gut include a high fiber diet with adequate water intake, lots of fruits and vegetables, whole grains, and an overall well-balanced diet. Another beneficial food is yogurt that contains active yeast and bacterial cultures as these have been shown to be supportive of the gut microbiome environment (Lee et al., 2022; Weersma et al, 2020).

The nurse should educate the client on ways to promote a healthy diet to ensure a balanced gut microbiota and homeostasis. Ways the nurse can educate the client to do achieve this include ensuring a high fiber intake with adequate water intake, lots of fruits and vegetables in the diet, lots of whole grains, restricting saturated fats, restricting alcohol, restricting sodium, and adding probiotic foods such as yogurts with active yeast and bacterial cultures.

Foods that can be included to increase fiber intake include foods such as fresh fruits and vegetables, lentils, kidney beans, split peas, chickpeas, quinoa, oats, popcorn, almonds, chai seeds, sweet potatoes, and dark chocolate. Foods that are high in fiber will help maintain the gut homeostasis, reduce cholesterol, promote healthy weight through satiety, reduce constipation by adding bulk to stool, and even reduce gastrointestinal cancer risks due to the antioxidant properties (Gunnars, 2020). Increasing fiber requires increasing water in the diet to ensure there is sufficient water in the stool to account for the added bulk in the stool.

Foods to restrict that are high in saturated fat include foods such as fatty cuts of meat, pies, sausage, butter, lard, cheddar cheese, ice cream, sour cream, biscuits, cakes, pastries, milk chocolate confectionaries, palm oil, coconut oil, and coconut cream (NHS, 2023). Saturated fats will not only cause imbalance in gut homeostasis, but they can raise bad cholesterol levels, increase colon cancer risks, promote arteriosclerosis, hypertension, and heart disease risks.

Foods to restrict that are high in sodium include foods such as breads and rolls, pizza, sandwiches, cold cuts and cured meats, soups, burritos and tacos, savory snacks like chips, popcorn, pretzels, snack mixes, and crackers, chicken, cheese, eggs and omelets (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2021). High amounts of sodium can upset the homeostasis of the gut by increasing dehydration risks and aid in hypertension risks.

Not only should the nurse educate the client on probiotics and a healthy diet to promote gut homeostasis and a healthy microbiome, they should also educate them on a class of foods known as prebiotics, those that feed the bacteria that are in the gut to promote their health (The President and Fellows of Harvard College, 2023). Foods that are in this class of foods includes things like garlic, onions, leeks, dandelion greens, seaweed, Jerusalem artichokes, asparagus, and bananas.

Pharmacology and Gut Health

Pharmacology and nutrition interact together as they both rely on the same processes to be available for use in the human body. When certain nutrients are lacking, nutrition is altered as well as the ability of certain drugs to be available for use in the body (Youdim, 2021). By the same token, the ability of the body to properly dispose of toxins from drugs and digestion wastes, are related and depend heavily on the overall health and nutritional state of the body. The nutritional state of a client greatly impacts the ability of their medications to be properly metabolized, effectively used, and rightly excreted. Nutritional status is extremely important to ensure medications for gut health can correctly function to support overall health.

There are medications that can help and hurt the microbiota of the gut and alter its homeostasis. A healthy microbiome also helps in digestion and absorption of medications, making a healthy gut important with regards to the best outcomes from treatments. Antibiotics have been a well-known culprit of altering the normal microbial growth in the gut. Long-term antibiotic use has been linked to Clostridium difficile infection and colon cancer. When a client is to be put on antibiotics for longer than three days, it is common practice that they also be given a probiotic to counteract some of the side effects the antibiotics will have on the gut microbiome in order to prevent infection.

Probiotics and Lactobacillus are two other well-known supplements that have an impact on the microbiome of the gut. The impact it creates is a positive and supportive one. It is encouraged that a client takes these when they have dietary practices or medications that will affect their gut homeostasis in a negative way. These need to be used in combination with a healthy supportive diet as they do not prevent all issues on their own; they simply add support to already healthy practices.

There are other medications that many people take that are not so well-known that impact the gut, yet they impact the gut in a negative way. Proton pump inhibitors for the treatment of gastroesophageal reflux disease, for example, decrease colonization of the microbiome and can lead to enteric infections like Clostridium difficile (Weersma et al., 2020). Metformin, used in the treatment of diabetes mellitus, increased the number of Escherichia coli in the intestines and lowered other bacterium, causing imbalances in the microbiome. Other medications that have been shown to disrupt the composition of microbe in the gut include statins for the treatment of hyperlipidemia, antidepressants, opioids for treatment of pain, and laxatives.
