Additive Manufacturing (AM), the industrial production term for 3D printing, is a relatively new form of advanced manufacturing. Advanced manufacturing in this context is any manufacturing technology that uses computing and or automation to accomplish a task. In the same way that the 3D printing you may be familiar creates an object by adding layer upon layer of plastic thread, AM adds layer upon layer of an “industrial strength” material to create a product. As the name suggests, it is distinguished by its ability to be additive. Rarely do you hear engineers discussing subtractive manufacturing, so why do we contrast legacy manufacturing with additive? And where does 3D printing come from? We will address these questions here.

Additive Manufacturing is a diverse field within the larger field of advanced manufacturing, which refers to the practice of using innovative technologies to create existing or new products. An advanced manufacturing process may not yet be fully mature, or have many users, but it shows promise (for example, virtual reality VR), speech recognition, or, in our case, AM. Another key aspect of AM is that it is disruptive, which is a heavy term to throw around, essentially means that the technology is innovative or groundbreaking, but this comes with a catch – disruption is not easy. If it were, it wouldn’t really be disruptive. The key disruptive aspect of AM is the layer-by-layer additive component, which is a novel form of creating an object or product.

AM Building Blocks

1.1 1.1 AM Building Blocks

Before we get into the anything more about AM, let’s go back to what we mean by traditional, or legacy manufacturing, which has often been “subtractive”. If you have ever carved something out of wood or used a lathe in a wood or metal shop, you have done a kind of subtractive manufacturing. Humans are quite good at creating products by subtractive manufacturing. For centuries, we have been carving things out of wood, stone, and metal. And we still do that now with other materials. From there, perhaps the material objects get formed, forged, pressed, shaped – all of which are very good manufacturing methods. In this chapter, we focus on where AM can be used to make legacy manufacturing operations faster, cheaper, and more efficient.

If 3D Printing is the act of making a 3-dimensional shape via a “printer”, then Additive Manufacturing is everything needed to make that shape into a “part”. In comparison, a forged or cast part would need to have a forging die (form of tooling) or casting pattern (another form of tooling) made beforehand to manufacture the shape required. In most forms of legacy manufacturing tooling is an essential component of the manufacturing process. Parts have requirements where form, fit and function are critical.

To make a 3D shape, the printer adds the material where it is needed, most likely in a 2-dimensional layer. The printer only concerns itself with a 2D layer at any point in time. It creates a stack of these layers full of 2-dimensional detail. The resulting 3-dimensional shape can be complex, have internal details, and won’t require tooling to make, since it and is generally computer controlled. This additive approach is a major shift in innovation from legacy manufacturing.

Machines Machines such as the 3D printers are easy to focus on, but our definition expands beyond the printers to include the machinery required to meet the industrial part requirements.

Materials Materials in the AM context includes all aspects of the materials used and how they are physically processed. We need to consider everything from material science, metallurgy, polymers, ceramics, welding, powder, and wire manufacture, to the finishing steps, and possibly heat treatment. Each aspect of how we manufacture the materials and the changes those materials will undergo both as a feedstock and then as a printed object is very important.

Additive Manufacturing Organization

Within Additive Manufacturing, each chapter stands on its own, so you can choose the order which best meets your need. Chapters are in a logical order according to the authors; however, it is understood that specific topics could be of more interest to some readers first, so starting with [link: Ch 06]AM Qualification and Certification[/link], for example, is acceptable. To absorb the complexity and comprehensive nature of AM today, however, all of the chapters are essential.

Readers need to understand that AM is diverse with process technologies. This diversity creates unique design spaces for shapes, sizes, materials, and mechanical performance. When we couple the mechanical operations with design, business, and qualification guidance, we need to seek a more overall comprehensive appreciation. For example, for a part to be qualified for an application like a structural part flying on an airplane, we must first understand the requirements for that part. These requirements likely include both technical and commercial considerations. These requirements will eliminate some of the AM processes as candidates due to size or speed. We then may need to consider what material is appropriate and the properties of that material in the AM process. Now we get into the design of the part and the design of the build which will critically determine the cost of the part as well as the strength. And while design for manufacturing (DFM) is not a new topic, designing for AM can be quite counterintuitive to those already versed in manufacturing and the design has an enormous impact on the viability as AM continues to mature. In this book, we weave together important topics to gain a strong understanding and comprehension of AM holistically and where AM is being used.

Machines

You will be introduced to machines in [link: Ch 02]Core AM Technologies and Supporting Processes[/link]. The topics covered range from the standard technical details of the various AM processes, how they work, and what is important to know about them. For example, more routine and familiar processing steps, like heat treatment, are critical to AM performance. Such processes may be employed exactly as they have been in the past or they may need to be optimized for AM products, materials and performance.

Device that manufacture AM parts are generally referred to as printers, and are similar to the laser or inkjet printer you might generate a report on. They are similar in that both create a 2-dimensional structure, but instead of printing on a new sheet of paper, the 3D printer indexes to a new level and prints the next layer of the part. Observing the printer in operation immediately enables a person to begin to get the concept of additive manufacturing. The printers come in many formats, brands, makes and models. There can even be multiple configurations of the same model, such as a single or dual laser. Today, any individual make and model has a sweet spot of operation and characteristics, therefore the requirements of the part you are making should help determine your machine choice. The machines are driven by build files, or digital instructions. The process development is again a function of the printer chosen and the part requirements.

Materials

In [link: Ch 03]AM Materials[/link], the various AM processes are examined as to the materials and material form to feed the AM processes. When AM was first arrived, the 3D complexity it offered was disruptive enough. Material choices were limited and performance was less important as AM was used for prototyping more than series production. Even though the technology is still in its infancy, we have many different materials to choose from, and we’re beginning to generate more specifications and performance data. As you might imagine, as processes such as metal AM mature are considered for applications such as medical implants and aerospace products, it is critical that we understand how the metallurgy and/or materials science works or is impacted. Researchers will continue to innovate and learn how to design for AM, and as they do, the materials will begin to drive the research toward higher performing and more affordable AM processes.

Digital

AM is a digitally intensive suite of technologies. [link: Ch 05]Designing for AM[/link] will discuss elements of the digital workflow including the Digital Thread and why designing for AM is so critical to business and technical success.

Within Designing for AM, we can further subdivide the design space into Design for AM (DfAM) and Modify for AM (MfAM). DfAM is typically associated with benefits of where there is a lot of design freedom. We refer to this as Opportunistic because we extoll benefits provided by the design of an AM part. MfAM is more associated with the restrictions or lack of design freedom and so we refer to this as Restrictive. MfAM tends to be more closely oriented with the AM process and its influence on the part.

Opportunistic reflects the limitless possibilities where the design can look like a plane that surely couldn’t fly, yet meets all of the requirements, with safe flight being only one of many. The solution space allows the consolidation of parts or incorporation of multifunction, such as supports that serve as cooling channels or a tool that both opens bottles and can be converted to a screwdriver. It is often a new part or a new design where there is less constraint coming from a current product.

Restrictive often deals with the realities of manufacturing so it heavily dependent on the AM process to be used. The orientation of the part and the build layout force the decision between shorter builds and fewer parts and longer builds with more parts. Other considerations include adding stock for a subsequent machining step and leaving datums for the machinist. MfAM is pervasive at all levels and is a companion to DfAM. An example is replacing a legacy manufacturing process like casting or making a spare part, where there is no option to change the design, and MfAM is the only option.

Harnessing the power of the digital workflow can go from very basic to very complex. A basic view is that we print an 3d design file of our choosing and look at the completed part. A very complex view adds the concept of the digital thread, or digital twin, where the 3D file is tracked as a build file and the final part can be compared both volumetrically and dimensionally to the original CAD version. All of this data is stored for retrieval later, if necessary.

The build file is more complex than a part or a shape. A file can be multiple parts whose orientation, angle, support and position in the build envelope help us not only meet the technical requirements but the commercial ones as well.

People

AM is a manufacturing technology that requires a mixture os skills in order for the implementation of it to be successful. A multidisciplinary team is needed because of the intertwining of design, materials, manufacturing and process engineering. In addition, the flexible nature of the technology can create new products and business models, which typically necessitates supply chain, accounting and even strategy to be discussed.

[Link: Ch 06]AM Certification and Qualification[/link] describes the certification and qualification process and related issues. Qualification and certification are very important to any industry. The people who manage, operate, and design the parts will make a critical difference in the degree of success. Therefore, it is important that they know that what is required of the process to make it to market is then critical commercially but equally important technically.

As you will learn in [link: Ch 09]The Business of AM[/link], the business of AM is dynamic and complex and still involves several process steps and a steep learning curve.

[link: Ch 08]AM Applications and Adoption Drivers[/link] and [link: Ch 09]The Business of AM[/link] merge the technical content with the business needs to underscore the interdisciplinary nature of AM, and to emphasize why it is critical to optimize the people within an organization. To better show this, we examine the economics of AM and tie it to the business of AM and how business cases can be made. The book concludes by exploring how AM can be applied, focusing on success factors, adoption drivers, barriers, overall technology adoption factors, and key AM supply chain roles.

Process Steps: Concept to Part

The best place to start thinking about using AM is at the concept stage. This stage is the most promising because the product requirements have not yet been finalized, and because AM enables complexity. The concept stage offers the first opportunity for an engineer to design to the product requirements before there are any constraints related to a specific manufacturing method. While thinking about using AM at the concept stage is ideal, it is usually not an option when an existing (i.e., already designed) part needs to be replaced.

Regardless of the AM process employed, the process requires a 3D file and follows a similar digital workflow. For example, the source for the future 3D object can be from Computer Aided Design (CAD) or from a 3D scanning source such as structured light, Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI), or Computed Tomography (CT). Once available in a 3D file, the source data will be turned into an STL file.

The STL file format, which is the most common file format in 3D printing, originated from stereolithography and can only describe the surface geometry of a three-dimensional object. It does not relay any other information, such as color, material, or texture. The STL file describes an the surface of the object using vertices of triangles placed on its surface. The normal of each triangle and a three-dimensional coordinate system fully defines the surface. STL files contain no scale information and have arbitrary units. While STL files are simple, they do not contain much information, which makes the task of tracking and documenting changes difficult. Consequently, researchers are exploring possible replacements. One approach, for example, is to stay in the native CAD format, which has the advantage that, if changes are made to the file for printing, they could be traced back to the design file.

The build file is the next step in the workflow. The build file is critical to ensure consistent results, expected results, economic results and compliance, and should be treated like a released engineering or manufacturing drawing. This file is much like forging die that is employed to manufacture parts over and over. With AM, the build file can be just a single part or part number but could also be multiple parts, part numbers, test coupons, etc. It is very important to remember that the original engineering design may or may not specify items like orientation during the build, but this a requirement for the build file.

From here our build file will be sliced into finite 2D layers to be processed one at a time by the AM system. Recall that the computers in 3D printing typically only concern themselves with a layer at a time, so each slice represents a build layer.

Once sliced, a tool path is generated, and the shape can be printed. This complete process is shown in Figure 1.4.

Figure 1.4
Figure 1.4 From CAD to physical part.

Post Processing refers to a host of manufacturing processes employed after the 3D printing step. These processes serve to finish an AM part by improving the appearance or material properties after it has been printed. These processes could be, but are not limited to, material removal, thermal processing, and surface treatments. The printed shape can be dimensionally measured to ensure consistency to the original drawing or source file.

Additive Manufacturing Processes

1.3 1.3 Additive Manufacturing Processes

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, students will be able to:

  • Define seven standard AM processes.
  • Explain the key similarities and differences between the standard AM processes.

Additive Manufacturing has matured into a diverse set of technologies all of which share an adding, or layer by layer, approach. While AM can be segmented into seven broad categories, innovation is occurring so rapidly that hybrids and combinations of AM processes are frequently being brought to market.

Any AM process can be described by answering just three questions: How is the layer created? How is the energy applied? How is the material applied? If you can describe the process with this simplified view of AM, you can easily move to the next layer of detail.

With the simplified view in mind, the seven broad methods employed in AM are:

Binder Jetting – A manufacturing process in which uses a liquid bonding agent deposited selectively to join powders.

  • Layer – The layer is made by spreading powder across a bed and stepwise lowering the bed defined by the slice thickness.
  • Energy – Two forms of energy are used: kinetic energy of the print ejectant, which is deployed through a print head and contains a polymer adhesive to bind the particles together. Thermal energy is used to cure the adhesive in subsequent steps. In metals and technical ceramics, a sintering or infiltration step is required.
  • Material – The materials used must be in powder form but may be metal, ceramic, or polymer.

Directed Energy Deposition – A processes in which materials are fused by an energy source as they are deposited. 

  • Layer – The layer is made by depositing one or more beads onto a substrate, which could be an existing part, with new deposits building either beside or on top of the previous beads.
  • Energy – Two forms of energy are used: Thermal energy is used to melt the material in a fusion DED process akin to traditional welding. In a solid-state DED process, kinetic energy accelerates powder particles to a high velocity splatting them into the previous layer, creating a solid-state metallurgical bond.
  • Material – The materials used may be in powder or wire form and are predominantly metal.

Material Extrusion – A process in which material is heated and delivered precisely through a nozzle or an orifice.

  • Layer – The layer is made by adding new deposits overtop the previous layer.
  • Energy – Two forms of energy can be used: Heat is applied to cause the material to flow through the nozzle. This is true for all materials. For metals, a sintering step is required for bound particles but not for friction-based processes.
  • Material – The materials used can be polymer and/or metal where the polymer is a filament and the metal is a particulate bound in the filament. The metal may also be a wire or particulate in the friction-based version.

Material Jetting – A process that selectively deposits droplets of build material through a print head.

  • Layer – The layer is made by selectively and precisely depositing the material in a desired location in an X-Y manner and then stepping is defined by the slice thickness.
  • Energy – Two forms of energy are used. Kinetic energy of the print ejectant is deployed through a print head and contains a fluid to deliver the target material. Thermal energy is used to cure the deposit. In metals, a sintering step is also required.
  • Material – The materials used can be polymer, metal, or ceramic in solution with a liquid delivery agent.

Powder Bed Fusion – A process in which thermal energy is used to fuse regions of a bed formed of powder material.

  • Layer – The layer is made by spreading powder across a bed and stepwise lowering the bed as defined by the slice thickness.
  • Energy – A beam of energy, such as a laser or electron mean, is used to fuse the particles together. 
  • Material – The materials used may be metal, ceramic, or polymer, but must be in powder form.

Sheet lamination - An additive manufacturing process in which sheets of material are layered and bonded

  • Layer – The layer is created by joining sheets of a material.
  • Energy – Flexible: ranges from a bonding agent to welding techniques such as ultrasonics.
  • Material – Sheets of polymer, metal, paper, and fibers

Vat Photopolymerization - An additive manufacturing process where a photo-reactive  liquid polymer is cured using light

  • Layer – The layer is created where the energy intersects the material to create the shape in a step wise fashion.
  • Energy – Focused energy in the form of a laser or focused light.
  • Material – Polymers, composites, and metals in photo curable polymers
  • Binder Jetting
  • Material Extrusion
  • Directed Energy Deposition
  • Material Jetting
  • Powder Bed Fusion
  • Sheet Lamination
  • Vat Photopolymerization

The AM Maturity Model

1.4 1.4 The AM Maturity Model

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, students will be able to:

  • Describe a maturity model for AM adoption.
  • Understand the technological and people requirements for AM adoption.

The Barnes Global Advisors (TBGA) pioneered the use of a maturity model to describe an approach to adopting AM throughout a product or design lifecycle. The TBGA AM Maturity Model, used throughout the book and shown in Figure 1.5, is a way to approach combining the product requirements to the skills and knowledge of AM. The scenarios on the Y axis describe increasing product or design complexity and therefore increasing product requirements. The X axis contemplates the necessary learning to be able to deploy, make, or manufacture a part to meet the requirements.

Figure 1.5
Figure 1.5 The Barnes Global Advisors AM Maturity Model describes levels of increasing product requirements and the skills and knowledge necessary to deploy AM at that level.

It is normal for one to see the culmination of an organization’s efforts in AM and want to replicate that success. Often, this success was built on years of development and trial and error. Jumping straight into making complex parts (e.g., Level 4) requires the highest level of skill and training. We’ll look at these levels in more depth, but it may help to think of this process as being analogous to running a marathon. In a marathon, a runner trains extensively to be prepared to finish or place in a marathon race. Without training, the runner introduces a high risk that could include injury or worse. The same sort of thing can happen with implementing AM into an organization in that it requires a deliberate and strategic approach that best meets the organization’s goals.

The TBGA AM Maturity Model contemplates both direct and indirect use of AM. At the lower levels, the indirect methods include prototypes (Level 0) and tooling (Level 1). In these efforts, AM is being deployed to support some other goal, but the AM part is not the part to be put in service. Prototyping and tooling are great places to begin an organization’s journey in AM because of the reduced requirements.

In the direct use of AM, the part made by AM is intended to go into service. This begins with replacing a legacy part with a part made by AM (Level 2) and is typically very restrictive from a design flexibility standpoint. Progressing to Level 3, the ability for AM to integrate complexity comes into play as several parts are unitized into a single assembly or part. At Level 4, the organization’s design, materials, people, and equipment knowledge is so extensive they can make a part with unrivaled performance that is not only complex and unitized but also is multi-functional. For example, this may be a structural part that carries a load but also serves to exchange heat or employ a sensor.

TBGA AM Maturity Model: Level 0

Parts made at this level are typically prototypes for product development or system testing. At this level, we may be interested in form, fit, or function but not all three. For example, we may want to make pieces for product development to help us assist us visualize how the product could be used. Alternatively, we may want to make AM pieces as stand ins for limited duration testing. Level 0 is a great place to gain experience with AM, because compared to higher levels the requirements are often not as tough, the risk is lower, and the learning opportunity is greater. At this level, it is expected you will start to explore the types of AM equipment and materials but neither may be critical to success.

TBGA AM Maturity Model: Level 1

As we continue the path of indirect parts, Level 1 use of AM is oriented at making tools or fixtures that are assistive in manufacturing or assembling the final system. Many organizations have found that making their own tooling has helped them save both time and money. In particular, it can reduce the cost of manufacturing for legacy processes that can have expensive or lengthy lead times for tooling, such as casting or reinforced polymer composites. The exploration of the machine type and mode narrows, and materials selection is more critical. In addition, traceability and/or configuration control increase in importance. You will also find that other entities like supply chain players become stakeholders in the outcome. Lastly, design thinking and AM thinking is starting to mature, as there is now more experience manufacturing components and using different types of machines.

TBGA AM Maturity Model: Level 2

At this level, we move into direct application of AM, where the created parts will enter service. At Level 2, the parts look very much like they did with legacy manufacturing methods. This could be due to many reasons such as AM being used to support spare parts, obsolescence, or a very restrictive design space where the ability to make changes are minimal. There could also be larger objectives to achieve, such as simply reducing risk by employing a new manufacturing method and seeing how different materials perform. Whatever the reason, Level 2 presents a significant challenge because the designer has little room to maneuver, and the design was likely optimized for a different process. Later, we will describe the restrictive and opportunistic design space with modify for AM (MfAM) and design for AM (DfAM), but at Level 2, MfAM skills are more dominant than DfAM.

The broader organization will become more involved in Level 2 as you will have to build a strategy for AM adoption and assess things like the supply chain since you are using a new technology, likely with new vendors, and/or new suppliers of materials or feedstock. Other important considerations to support manufacturing may include inspection, material specifications, process specifications and design allowables. The supply chain implications are discussed in more depth in [link: Ch 08]AM Applications and Adoption Drivers[/link].

TBGA AM Maturity Model: Level 3

At this level, the AM skills are becoming more critical. As parts are being consolidated and joints or interfaces are being eliminated, likely a structural efficiency benefit is being sought. This efficiency will lead to weight savings or better performance and endurance.

Financially, the AM part will likely have fewer manufacturing steps and decreased assembly time, thus collapsing the Bill of Material (BOM), drawing count, vendors, and touch labor to assemble.

DfAM skills are now quite high, and you will understand that you may now have to start making design considerations for the additive manufacturing process. For example, if you redesign the part and make it too light, other considerations will come into play.

The organization will continue to lean in to support the design and manufacture; computers and algorithms can mathematically design and optimize, but they have no consideration for practical aspects, such as a part’s surrounding environment. MfAM is still very relevant when it comes time to print the part successfully or meet the final part dimensional requirements by adding stock during printing, for example, to be able to machine to final tolerances.

TBGA AM Maturity Model: Level 4

At this Level, a part can only be made economically through use of AM. There are fewer examples of Level 4 parts today due to the merging of the need for world-class DfAM, supply chain support, marketing awareness, and general management support to acknowledge the need to integrate organizational awareness. The Level 4 part will have unrivaled performance and will likely impact the system it sits within, thus driving the need for organization-wide AM awareness. There will be a need to fully appreciate the system-level tradeoffs in design for additive manufacturing.

AM Rationale and Selection Processes

1.5 1.5 AM Rationale and Selection Processes

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, students will be able to:

  • Identify common reasons for using additive manufacturing.
  • Describe the requirements for choosing an AM process.

There are numerous reasons that people and organizations employ AM. Typically, we refer to the Project Management Triple Constraint of Schedule, Scope (or Performance), and Cost as the fundamental considerations. Digging further into these three constraints, there are many sub-elements to consider.

  • Schedule – Reducing production time or working inventory, compressing test or product development cycles, printing tools versus legacy means.
  • Scope – Improving system and/or part performance through complexity or part count reduction, added functionality, improving durability, reducing CO2 emissions.
  • Cost – Reducing cost of parts by improving assembly time and/or repeatability, reducing and/or expediting tooling time, design for AM improvements in part cost, reducing the number of parts that need to be produced, reducing the touch labor and processes in assembly, reducing cost to produce generally, and increasing revenue through enhanced performance.

To expand the Triple Constraints further, let’s explore some additional characteristics of AM design (see Figure 1.6). Many organizations turn to AM for Speed, for example by eliminating the time required to produce tooling. This can reduce manufacturing cost, as explained in the following discussion of design characteristics. Complexity, in traditional manufacturing is synonymous with cost, the more complex something is the more costly it is to manufacture. With AM complexity often costs significantly less because the manufacturer only needs to produce what is required for the application. By building up only what is needed, much of the waste of legacy manufacturing of starting from a block of material and removing it can be eliminated and less material is used. This concept discussed further in this section.

Speed

A key characteristic of Additive Manufacturing is that it improves time to market. AM provides a fast track from concept to production, where complex objects can be manufactured in a single process step. As a result, product innovations are designed, developed, and tested more rapidly, which eliminates the need for expensive and time-consuming prototype fabrication.

Cost

Inevitably, designers and engineers learn that taking weight out of a part leads to faster printing or faster build times. With traditional machining, more time on the mill often means lighter parts, but it costs more because of the time it takes to produce the part. With additive manufacturing, as we are adding material in a layer wise fashion, we think about weight and cost differently.

Complexity

Complexity simply means that, with AM, parts can be designed with more complexity than they were before. As with all things, however, there are limitations. These parts can more closely follow a load path and, therefore, be lighter or more efficient in carrying load or stiffness.

Design

AM can offer some significant differences in terms of design, whether it’s functional or performative. For example, sometimes “design" means adding design details in the form of functional features like cooling channels for tooling. In this case, complex passages can be utilized, because there is no drilling required; therefore, you can manage the critical elements such as turbulent flow and pressure drops. Such a design change can lead to performance advantages when truly optimized. Other performance advantages allow the designer to include features that would otherwise have to be added in a secondary operation.

Durability

The durability of parts can be enhanced primarily by providing a solid, single-material component, as opposed to having to eliminate mating parts or surfaces. When we eliminate weldments, brazes, fasteners, etc., as create structural inefficiency. Instead, when we combine multiple parts via AM – known as unitization - , the product is more efficient and therefore more durable.

Increasing durability is also possible through local or bulk additions of new material. For example, repairing, locally supporting, or adding material has the benefit of making parts that may come in contact with other parts more durable. In some cases, the ability to add material to a specific location efficiently will improve the longevity of the part.

Light Weighting

The ability to make parts lighter than their legacy designs is one of the most exciting elements for engineers when first introduced to AM. Because we are adding material layer by layer, we can achieve lightweight structures. Conventional manufacturing is largely subtractive, which, paradoxically, means that as we continue to remove material, we add cost. In AM, you don’t pay for material that you don’t add.

Social

AM benefits both society and the environment. When we use less material and fewer operations, we can reduces energy consumption, transportation, and logistics costs, all of which have a direct benefit on the environment primarily through the reduction of CO2 emissions.

AM Process Selection

Choosing the right AM process can be a difficult process, especially given all the variables and diversity of AM. However, a focus on requirements will always help focus the decision. Inherently, there is logical approach which can be quite challenging to get good answers to resolve. It will always be a question of commercial and technical requirements. Commercially, we are interested in things like the annual demand or throughput of parts, cost versus the legacy manufacturing method, lead time, supply chain maturity and risk. Technically, the overall size of the part, the detail resolution, the material, part performance, inspection needs, and surface finish factor into which AM process is suitable.

The combination of commercial and technical requirements creates a picture of what has to be true to deploy AM as a method. In one instance, for example, it may be as simple as needing a specific material and there are only one or two methods that use that material. In other cases, the overall size of the part will dictate the process selection. Alternatively, manufacturing speed and delivery of parts could be a key driver, as AM processes today is slow compared to legacy manufacturing counterparts.

Interdisciplinary Considerations for AM Decisions

Just as AM process selection requires input from multiple groups within an organization, it is imperative that an AM project is also staffed with employees who have an appropriate skills mix. Because AM encompasses multiple fields of materials ranging from polymers to metals and ceramics, team members need to be knowledgeable in these areas. For example, manufacturing specialists will be interested in feedstock and process sensitivities. Design engineers, on the other hand, can design for complexity but they need to consider which AM process is going to be used, as well as the associated tradeoffs.

Alternatively, if the program managers, supply chain professionals and leadership aren’t aware enough to understand what can be achieved with AM, maximum value will not be achieved. AM is best used when it can be brought in at the concept level and left as a miracle solution after other approaches have failed.

For all AM projects, the technical community needs to be engaged early and work with manufacturing, supply chain, and program management, which ultimately calls on the leadership to create an environment where the organization can be successful with AM. All parties mentioned above must be present at some point, otherwise the risk of the AM project being implemented increases.

How AM is Used Today

AM found early adopters in several industries, including the Aerospace, Medical, and Defense sectors. These sectors are distinct as they have a high mix and low volume of parts, meaning they make a low number of many different parts. Consequently, creating tooling for low volume parts is un-economical because the tooling amortization becomes a significant component of the per part cost, but is often hard to avoid.

A wide range of other industries also realized the benefit of creating personalized or bespoke products with AM. For example, in the luxury goods and jewelry sectors professionals realized that personalized products could be sold at a premium price. Similarly, professionals in dentistry began to understand that dental applications , which need to be customized for each individual, were a natural fit for AM. A prime example of this is the Invisalign dental aligner. Energy sectors have been researching AM for years as they share some similar characteristics to aerospace and in some cases even similar materials. High value, low part quantities drive the Energy sector to avoid tooling solutions when possible.

Most recently, AM has been successfully used in the automotive and heavy transportation industries. In automotive industries, for example, several factors are converging and driving intense interest in AM:

  1. Material substitution has been used for the last several decades; steel to aluminum, aluminum to plastic. Today, simple substitution has been exhausted and the only way to get further weight out is through re-designing the component.
  2. Electric vehicles need lighter structures to counter the mass of the batteries and electric motors.
  3. Higher end cars, such as Formula 1, can use AM to make specialized, customized components that improve performance and thus increase profit.

Another benefit of AM is that it can reduce the need to store large inventories. As such, many industries are exploring ways to create digital, rather than physical, warehouses wherever possible. Their goal is to avoid keeping and paying for inventories of costly spare parts, especially considering that some parts may become obsolete.

Like any innovative, disruptive technology that takes time to mature and become more wildly adopted, AM is evolving in different ways and at different rates within the industries that are using it. This kinetic mindset of AM is important to keep in mind as you progress through the book. The following “Four Ds” can help you remember and frame new AM technologies and improvements.

  • Disruptive – AM doesn’t work like previous methods of manufacturing. It is additive by inception, which requires different conceptual thinking and design rules.
  • Diverse – AM is a family of technologies, but if you learn the Simplified View of AM: Layer, Energy and Material, then you can likely describe any process.
  • Deployed – How an organization deploys AM is important. The TBGA AM Maturity Model provides a conceptual roadmap to build skills and grow the organizational knowledge to successfully adopt AM.
  • Developing – Along with the how you deploy AM, the Four Building Blocks create a successful construct to reduce risk and friction within an organization as AM is inherently interdisciplinary.

As you choose your own path through the topics, these tools will help you gain a strategic and tactical foundation to succeed. AM is dynamic and rapidly growing, so our mission with this book is to arm you with tools and knowledge to succeed today and prepare you for whatever may come tomorrow.
