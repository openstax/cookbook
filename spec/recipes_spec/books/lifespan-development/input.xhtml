About Lifespan Development

Lifespan Development is designed to support both semesters of the world history course offered at both two-year and four-year institutions. Serving a student base of both majors and non-majors in the field, and an institutional variation in requirements of one or two semesters depending on the plan of study, the course introduces students to a global perspective of history couched in an engaging narrative. Concepts and assessments help students think critically about the issues they encounter so they can broaden their perspective of global history and how the topics studied apply to their current life as citizens of the world.

Coverage and Scope

Lifespan Development is designed to support the one-semester, introductory-level developmental psychology course offered at both two- and four-year institutions. This course is a requirement across many institutional departments, serving a student base of psychology majors plus students majoring in nursing, education, sociology, and other healthcare and science disciplines. The course introduces learners to core concepts and classic and current research on human development. Features and assessments help students think critically about the issues they encounter so they can broaden their perspective of lifespan development and apply the topics studied to their current and future personal and professional lives.

Lifespan Development consists of 17 chapters. As previously noted, the content will be presented in a chronological format, which research has shown produces better depth of understanding and application, as well as better long-term retention (as opposed to a topical format). Chronological organization appeals to the largest market segment and better facilitates a biopsychosocial understanding of developmental psychology for most students. Within the chronological structure, the physical, cognitive, social, and emotional aspects of each developmental stage will be addressed separately. This “substructure” will allow instructors who are accustomed to teaching the course in a topical format to easily identify topics for presentation and discussion.

Lifespan Development opens with a chapter on lifespan psychology and major theories and basic science of human development. The first chapter also introduces research methods used in the field. Subsequent chapters address the lifespan from prenatal development to death. Each chapter will discuss key developmental milestones and the wide range of variance in human development as influenced by genetics, culture, environment, and other factors. For each developmental stage, one chapter covers physical and cognitive aspects, while a second chapter covers social and emotional aspects. The final chapter discusses end of life, death, and grieving.

Engaging feature boxes

Throughout Lifespan Development, you will find features that engage students by taking selected topics a step further and challenge students to apply what they are learning. Each feature box contains either a link to a deeper exploration of the topic at hand or critical thinking questions that may be geared toward class discussion, student projects, or written essays.   • Have You Heard? Students are presented with this feature at the beginning of each chapter.   • Life Hacks. This feature provides specific guidance on how students can apply the material in the chapter to improve their own lives and the lives of others (e.g., tips on parenting, resolving conflict, caring for elders).

[H2]Brain Changes in Middle Adulthood

What happens to our brain now that it is fully formed? Just because the growing process has stopped does not mean the brain has ceased developing too. There is a great deal of stability in brain during middle adulthood and aspects that are indistinguishable from that of early adult such as the ability to make and utilize mental images (Cacola, et al., 2013). Researchers have identified differences in other areas though, some of which show improved functioning and some of which show less functioning, as shown in [link].

The brain reflects overall more stable emotions in middle adulthood. Findings suggest middle age adults are better at focusing on positive information and less amygdala reactivity to negative information as compared to younger adults (Maher et al., 2004). It is not uncommon to hear adults in middle age comment about becoming forgetful and even presenting for memory assessments in middle age. This is not consistent with research and often times the perceived problems in memory are a result of stress and anxiety. In fact, differences in memory between middle and young adults are not typically found and seem to generally function at the same level (Salthouse, 2012).

[H2]Sensation and Perception in Middle Adulthood

Just as people experience changes to their brain and body, sensory abilities also begin to experience changes during middle adulthood. Structural components of the eyes change. Hearing acuity recedes from primary and secondary aging. Even other senses experience subtle changes during this time. As seen in [link], the eyes experience a loss of visual acuity in middle age referred to as presbyopia. This results from a thickening of the lens which reduces the amount of light that can reach the retina. Two common issues arise from presbyopia. As the thickening occurs it reduces the elasticity of the eye causing it to become more farsighted. A reason many adults rely on reading glass and bifocals to increase visual acuity when reading. Additionally, a yellow material builds up from the thickened lens reducing light. The limitation of light influences the comfort of night driving. Drivers experiencing presbyopia may have difficulty reading street signs at night or become more disrupted by the headlights of other cars. Night driving is additionally complicated by a significant loss of rods in middle adulthood (Grossniklaus et al., 2013). A further issue with lens thickening is that it puts more strain on the on the muscles around the eye making it more difficult to focus on objects.

[H3]Presbycusis

Age related hearing loss is known as presbycusis. Statistics vary widely on the percent of middle-aged adults with hearing loss, however it is safe to say it begins to affect many during this time. Over time the inner ear’s structures and nerves slowly deteriorate. Initial hearing loss begins at the extremities with high frequency sounds. Secondary causes of hearing loss such as noise exposure are also common. Men tend to experience more hearing loss than women do which maybe an aspect of working in professions or hobbies with more noise exposure. For example, the second most common disability benefit to US service members is hearing loss with 1.3 million receiving compensation (Veteran Benefit Administration, 2020). This is only topped by tinnitus, another hearing related condition. Hearing aids can be beneficial to those struggling with hearing loss. However, many avoid or put off compensating their hearing due to hassle or cost (Li-Korotky, 2012). Those who do choose hearing aids may find that it reduces rates of cognitive declines and even dementia risk (Yeo et al., 2022).

Philosophers from Aristotle to John Locke believed in the notion of tabula rasa or we are born into this world as blank slates, waiting to be crafted and molded by experience. Behavioral psychologists have agreed with this perspective, with John B. Watson having stated, “Give me a dozen healthy infants, well-formed, and my own specified world to bring them up in and I’ll guarantee to take any one at random and train him to become any type of specialist I might select.”

Although experience and environment certainly shape our lives, infants come into this world as their own unique individuals. Temperament is the term used to describe the innate, biological components of individuality that are present shortly after birth. Temperamental traits are patterns of behavior that are consistent and relatively stable throughout infancy. Rudolf Krause defined it thusly:

"The skull and brain belonged to a boy who was born on the 4th of October, 1869, the last of four children. Paul was scrofulous from his youth. He did not get his teeth until the end of his second year, and they were quite brown in color and were soon lost. According to the statement of Paul's mother, he had several successive sets of teeth. It was not until the fifth year that he learned to walk. He was cleanly from the third year, but not when he felt ill. His appetite was always good up to his last sickness of four weeks. His sleep was habitually undisturbed. He was of a cheerful temperament, and inclined to play; as soon as he heard music he would dance, and sing to the music in rather unmelodious tones. When teased he could be very violent; he would throw anything he could lay his hands on at the head of the offender. He liked the company of others, especially of men. By the time he was four years old he had learned to eat without help."

–Rudolf Krause

Mary Rothbart wrote of three temperamental traits that had strong biological ties: 1) surgency, 2) negative affectivity, and 3) effortful control. Surgency refers to the amount of motor activity and arousal that an infant experiences and is expressed through physical activity, sociability, and sensation seeking. In comparison, negative affectivity is associated with biological reactivity and self-regulation and is expressed through an infant’s distress, discomfort, and avoidance behavior ([link]Figure 07_06_Correlations[/link]). Finally, effortful control is linked with attention and inhibition, and the ability to persist and persevere on tasks. According to Rothbart, different combinations of these traits are possible. For instance, an infant who is high in both surgency and negative affectivity would be prone to anger, but an infant low in surgency but high in effortful control may enjoy quiet, enduring tasks such as puzzles and cognitive toys (Shiner et al., 2012).

Although temperamental traits are biologically innate in infancy, there are environmental and contextual factors that can also play a role. For instance, our temperamental expression may be increasing influenced by our environment as we age (McCall, 1987). How parents react, encourage, or respond to infants can modify their temperamental expressions. Sometimes, this can help to reinforce and encourage an infants’ innate temperamental trait, and sometimes parenting behavior can discourage expression of a trait.

[H2] Imitation

Involuntary imitation is an important bonding mechanism for young infants. Andrew Meltzhoff examined infant involuntary imitation which is the reflexive instinct for infants to match caregivers’ facial expressions. Young infants between the ages of 1 and 4 months have no voluntary control over this form of imitation. Instead, the mirror neurons in the infants’ brain allows them to replicate a variety of facial expressions such as smiles, smirks, and tongue protrusions. Through mirroring and matching a caregiver, involuntary imitation strengthens emotional bonds.

By the age of 8 to 12 months, infants can display voluntary imitation in which they can imitate the gestures and facial expression of another person. Voluntary imitation is conscious and controllable, but limited to only immediate imitation at this age. A one year old infant is unable to imitate something they saw hours or days in the past.

Along with voluntary imitation, one year old infants also display social referencing. Social referencing is the tendency to look to others during novel situations to understand how to feel and respond. In unusual or ambiguous situations, infants tend to look to the facial expression of their caregiver to interpret and respond to the experience. If a caregiver looks calm or happy, infants are more likely to interpret a novel situation as safe and exciting. If a caregiver looks nervous, upset, fearful, or angry, infants are more likely to become nervous, timid, or avoidant as shown in [link].

Social referencing can be especially helpful during times of joint attention, or when an infant followers a caregiver’s body language or voice to attend to an intended stimuli. When a caregiver points to a toy or an animal, an infant is able to understand and follow their caregiver’s gaze and gesture. Joint attention requires the ability to understand the perspective of another person, which is the basis of social cognition, or understanding how or what others are thinking.

Over the first year of life, infants develop voluntary imitation, social referencing, and joint attention skills through constant attention and focus on their caregivers. The role of the caregiver is essential to healthy social development of infants and toddlers, particularly as it comes to infant attachment.

The impact of families and wider communities on infant social development can be viewed through Uri Bronfenbrenner’s ecological model as seen in Figure_04_05_Ecological. According to this model, children are nested within a microsystem, which is the direct, regular people in their lives. Parents who interact hostile or lovingly with each other in front of their infant make up this level of influence.

Moving beyond family, the ecological model’s next level is the exosystem which refers to indirect influence through community norms, neighborhood trends, and extended family environment. Living in a neighborhood in which neighbors are friendly and supportive towards one another or merely strangers influences this level. In addition, neighborhood and community safety, activities, and resources are also components of the exosystem.

Next is the macrosystem which describes broad influences of culture, such a geography, religion, nationality, and language. Living in a culture that observes democratic voting and elections as compared to a culture that is represented by an appointed monarchy, or in a culture that provides free health care versus one in which health care is costly to families are features of the macrosystem.

Finally, there is the chronosystem which describes major historical events that happen during an infant’s life that will impact their development, such as being young during a global pandemic when many families worked from home or growing up in times of war or natural disaster. What Type of Childcare is Best?

With so many options for childcare, parents can be overwhelmed with navigating what is best for their family. Traditionally, many families relied on family care, so it feels familiar and a great way to improve family bonds and pass along cultural values. Temperature

Three temperature scales are in general use:

  • Fahrenheit (F); water freezes at 32 °F and boils at 212 °F.
  • Celsius or [footnote] (C); water freezes at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C.
    Celsius is now the name used for centigrade temperature; it has a more modern standardization but differs from the old centigrade scale by less than 0.1°. WW-test notes that this is a footnote.
  • Kelvin or absolute (K); water freezes at 273 K and boils at 373 K.

All molecular motion ceases at about -459°F=-273°C=0K-459°F=-273°C=0K, a temperature called absolute zero. Kelvin temperature is measured from this lowest possible temperature, and it is the temperature scale most often used in astronomy. Kelvins have the same value as centigrade or Celsius degrees, since the difference between the freezing and boiling points of water is 100 degrees in each. (Note that we just say “Kelvins,” not Kelvin degrees.)

On the Fahrenheit scale, the difference between the freezing and boiling points of water is 180 degrees. Thus, to convert Celsius degrees or Kelvins to Fahrenheit degrees, it is necessary to multiply by 180/100=9/5180/100=9/5. To convert from Fahrenheit degrees to Celsius degrees or Kelvins, it is necessary to multiply by 100/180=5/9100/180=5/9.
