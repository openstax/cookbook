About World History

World History is designed to support both semesters of the world history course offered at both two-year and four-year institutions. Serving a student base of both majors and non-majors in the field, and an institutional variation in requirements of one or two semesters depending on the plan of study, the course introduces students to a global perspective of history couched in an engaging narrative. Concepts and assessments help students think critically about the issues they encounter so they can broaden their perspective of global history and how the topics studied apply to their current life as citizens of the world.

Coverage and scope

The World History resource consists of 32 chapters, with a “split” at 1500 to align with the common division of the course across two semesters. The Table of Contents begins with a chapter on the study of history, explicitly addressing the nuances of perspective and interpretation, how those have informed the canon familiar to many students, and how students can broaden their knowledge and learning through a global perspective that is aware of the evolution of how history is studied and an appreciation for how it is relevant today.

The first half of the text then introduces Part I, starting with human evolution and proceeding through early societies. Part II covers city-states and empires in the Mediterranean region and explores the Americas and Africa in ancient times. The role of religion in historical development is examined in Part III. The first half of the course closes with Part IV’s coverage of the middle ages across the globe, including the Mongol, Ghanian, Malian, and Ottoman Empires, and the impact of climate change, the Black Plague, and the adoption of gunpowder. A brief closing chapter provides a framework to wrap up the discussion to 1500 and to contextualize the post-Columbus content that will begin the second half. This bridge will serve to help students understand that the conquests that follow were not an inevitability.

The second half of the text spans the period from roughly 1500 CE to the present day. Part V examines the emergence of a global world via exchange and trade between regions. Starting around 1750 and moving into the 20th century, Part VI begins with colonization, teaches about revolutions in Europe, North America, and Latin America, and proceeds into chapters on expansion and labor resulting in the industrial world. Part VII spans to the present day with coverage on both world wars, the interwar period, and the Cold War, and closes with a chapter on globalization and the future. The final chapter addresses a global economy, technology, and social and environmental issues facing the world.

Engaging Feature Boxes

Throughout World History , you will find features that engage students by taking selected topics a step further and challenge students to apply what they are learning. Each feature box contains either a link to a deeper exploration of the topic at hand or critical thinking questions that may be geared toward class discussion, student projects, or written essays. Our features include:

  • In Their Own Words. Students are presented with a textual primary source for review and/or analysis. Depending on the source, they may be asked a series of questions that develop the students’ ability to think critically about primary sources.
  • Dueling Voices. This feature presents either a brief historiographical debate on the topic at hand that highlights history as an interpretive discipline, or a side-by-side primary source reading that offers two different interpretations or perspectives of the same event.
  • Beyond the Book. Students engage with non-textual sources such as art, physical objects, or architecture, with the goal of understanding the value of these kinds of sources in historical work.
  • The Past Meets the Present. This is a point of connection or relevancy piece that highlights an aspect of chapter content that speaks to an issue in today’s world.
  • Link to Learning This feature provides online resources and videos that are pertinent to students’ deeper exploration of the topics. Link to Learning boxes allow students to connect easily to some of the most important thought leaders and concepts in the field.

Some feature boxes include questions for further exploration. Connections and Finding Your Voice are assignment or discussion launching pads that appear and the end of some feature boxes. “Connections” encourage students to make connections across different historical time periods and/or geographic areas. “Finding Your Voice” asks students to apply what they’ve just learned via a “choose your own adventure” type of assignment that builds on what they have explored in the feature box; this may be presented as an online discussion forum, an in-class activity or presentation, or a group project.

End-of-chapter materials to assess and extend student learning

Each chapter concludes with a series of assessments covering the content and learning objectives of each section. The variety of question types provides a robust opportunity for students to explore what they’ve learned and practice the associated critical thinking skills relevant to the study of history. Additionally, assessments are designed to allow instructors options for courses taught in-person, virtually, or via a hybrid approach.

  • Review Questions. Multiple-choice assessments that test for conceptual understanding of key concepts.
  • Check Your Understanding. Short-answer questions where students explain concepts in words.
  • Reflection Questions. Short-answer assessments that ask students to reflect on concepts.
  • Visual-Based Questions. Short-answer questions that prompt student thinking around maps, charts, or graphs.
  • Making Connections. Long-answer questions that require students to compare and contrast topics or regions, across or within time periods.
  • Thought Provokers. Written assignments that require more complex analyses and potentially use case studies.
  • Source Connection. Assessment that asks students to read and consider a new primary source and relate it to a topic in the chapter.

Africa and Migration

In The Descent of Man (1871), the English naturalist Charles Darwin proposed that Africa was the “cradle of humankind.” At the time, Darwin’s suggestion was highly controversial, in part because it flew in the face of accepted belief in the Biblical creation story of mankind, and because it challenged European notions of racial superiority. But a raft of discoveries in the 20th century, including Australopithecus afarensis by Raymond Dart in South Africa, the stone-tool-wielding Homo habilis by the Leakey family in Tanzania’s Olduvai Gorge, and “Lucy,” the most famous of all Australopithecine specimens, by Donald Johanson in Ethiopia’s Awash River Figure 1.2, have revealed a fossil record that confirms Darwin’s theory of humankind’s African genesis. Recent DNA mapping of human descent has corroborated the earlier painstaking work of these paleoanthropologists.

The appearance of H. habilis between 2.3 and 1.65 million years ago signaled the beginning of the Stone Age, an epoch in human history divided into three periods: the Early, Middle, and Late. Homo sapiens, the ancestor of modern humans (Homo sapiens sapiens), diverged from these earlier hominin species during the Middle Stone Age, around 200,000 years ago. Recognizably modern humans appeared in the savannah woodlands of eastern and southern Africa a hundred or so millennia later during the Late Stone Age or Neolithic Period, sometime between 120,000 and 90,000 years ago. The Neolithic was witness to a series of dramatic technological and social revolutions that transformed humanity, in particular the domestication of plants and animals, farming, and the invention of pottery. Although these advances developed gradually, and often in response to each other, the result was a cumulative package of human innovation referred to as the Neolithic Revolution.

Before the Neolithic Revolution, life in prehistoric Africa was characterized primarily by the hunter-gatherer stage of human civilization. Hunter-gatherers survived directly from nature through the gathering of wild roots and fruits and vegetables, as well as the hunting of wild animals. Communities of hunter-gatherers were very small, typically consisting—at most—of several dozen people. Despite their size, these groups had contact and engaged in limited trade with other similar populations who, like themselves, were highly mobile. The unsettlement of these peoples was necessitated by their circumstances: limited resource availability, the need for water, the reality of natural calamities and human conflict all forced these groups to live transportable and peripatetic lifestyles. The small group size also meant little hierarchy and informal authority, although the earliest forms of role specialization are apparent at this stage of human development: younger and heartier members of the community were assigned more strenuous duties than the elderly; hunting was the preserve of menfolk; gathering was more typically the role of womenfolk and children. The Long Peace

The agricultural and pastoral innovations of the Neolithic Revolution came together in the period after 10,000 B.C.E. and brought with them important changes to the way of life of many of the Stone Age peoples of Africa. For the first time, the domestication of plants and animals—or “farming”—enabled fixed settlement, resulting in the abandonment of hunter-gatherer nomadism by all but a few African groups. Although scholars still debate its origins, the oldest evidence of agriculture and pastoralism comes from northeastern Africa where, sometime after 9000 B.C.E., the Nilo-Saharans adopted the grain-collecting and grinding techniques of their northern neighbors and applied them to sorghum and pearl millet, the tropical grasses of the middle Nile. By around 8000 B.C.E., they had invented pottery to cook a grain-based oatmeal-like dish. During the same period, the Nilo-Saharans domesticated the wild cattle of the Red Sea hills, developing herding and grazing techniques that, like their agricultural practices, gradually diffused westward across the Sahara and northward toward Egypt, as shown in Figure 1.3.

The spread and adoption of new agricultural and pastoral techniques as well as iron technology in Africa was slow and uneven, affected to varying degrees by a range of circumstances. In the case of agriculture and pastoralism, environmental factors (such as rainfall and soil fertility) and biological factors (including the prevalence of the tsetse fly and lactose intolerance among human populations, both of which prevented the keeping of cattle) predetermined suitability. Nevertheless, agriculture was adopted successfully in most areas of Africa, often despite poor soil, and manifested first in West and Central Africa and, later, in East and Southern Africa. The coincidence of ironworking with agriculture shares a similar geography. In North, West, and Central Africa, farming generally preceded iron, while in East and Southern Africa, agriculture and iron spread nearly simultaneously.

As it happened, these developments largely overlapped with—and contributed to—the rapid spread of a family of some 400-500 closely related languages known as Bantu. Around five thousand years ago (ca. 3000 B.C.E.), the ancestors of the Bantu (the “proto-Bantu”) were centered on the area between modern-day Nigeria and Cameroon in West-Central Africa. A Stone Age people, the proto-Bantu were agriculturalists who subsisted by cultivating pearl millet and yams and extracting oil from the abundant palm and bush candle (Canarium) trees of the region’s luxuriant rainforests. Gradual changes in weather patterns caused the rainforests to recede and, coupled with increasingly seasonal rainfall, opened tracts of savanna between forested areas. Over time, these open patches merged to form the Sangha River Interval, a 200-mile-wide grassland running north-south between modern-day southeastern Cameroon, southern Central African Republic, and northern Republic of Congo. This grassland corridor allowed the hitherto forest-dwelling Bantu to move southward, through what had once been impenetrable tropical rainforest.

The movement of the Bantu-speaking people beginning around 2000 B.C.E. is referred to as the Bantu Migrations. Although scholars continue to debate the precise timing, motivation, and directions of expansion, linguistic and archaeological evidence in the form of pottery and ironmaking technology suggest that there were multiple waves of Bantu migrations. The earliest phase seems to have consisted of two phases: an initial eastern stream or “early split,” followed somewhat later by a western “rivers and coasts” stream. Family Life in Rome

Family life was oriented around the paterfamilias, the male head of the household. According to tradition, this patriarch had the power of life and death over all his dependents, an authority referred to as patria potestas. Members of the extended family subject to this authority included the patriarch’s wife, children, anyone descended through the family’s male line, and all slaves belonging to the household. With his authority, the patriarch was both the judge and rule maker of the family, with the power to sell his dependents into bondage or destroy their property.

The Spark That Lit the Powder Keg

Despite these extreme powers, the goal of the paterfamilias was to promote his family’s welfare. Ultimately, a father’s power worked through consensus and deliberation with the other members of the family.

In early Rome, children were educated in the home; later, grammar schools enrolled boys and girls from wealthy families until around the age of twelve. Though more elite families could afford private tutors, education for Romans usually centered on reading and writing Latin and Greek as well as arithmetic. Around age fifteen, boys would don the toga virilis (“toga of manhood”), a plain white toga representing their enrollment as citizens and entrance into manhood. Sons of prominent families could then go on to a civil or military career. Since men would eventually inherit their fathers’ property (as well as their debts), they were compelled to continue their family’s prosperity.

By contrast, girls commonly married at a young age, most between fourteen and eighteen years old, and often to much older men. In the most common form of marriage, a wife entered a marriage with a dowry that became the husband’s property. Thus, a woman from a wealthy background with a large dowry had some sway in making marriage arrangements. But less well-off women were more reliant on their husbands’ status. In any case, marriage represented a woman coming under the legal control of her husband’s household.

This vast legal and age imbalance between husband and wife resonates with the cultural restrictions on Roman women (Figure 1.40). Women were responsible for the management of the household, including ensuring provisions for the family, overseeing the slaves and other dependents, and looking after the children. Spinning wool was viewed as the activity of an ideal Roman woman, and many wives were expected to occupy themselves with this work. Despite these expectations, there is evidence that many women held professions outside the home, including in medicine, trade, and agriculture.

Romans lived and worked in a variety of contexts across the empire. Most of our evidence for understanding the practical elements of Roman daily life comes from archaeological evidence at Pompeii. This bustling Italian city shows us the occupations, architecture, and lifestyles of different social classes. In addition, though most of our evidence concerns wealthy estates, life outside the city in the countryside was an important part of Roman society.

Daily life was dominated by aristocratic men who enjoyed careers in politics, law, and the military. Social relationships centered on these upper-class patrons and their clients; every morning patrons would receive a number of clients in their homes seeking aid in exchange for loyalty. Afterward, a man’s late morning usually involved responsibilities outside the home including business and political meetings. During the afternoon, wealthy Roman men would spend their time socializing and pursuing leisure activities, such as attending public entertainment performances or visiting the bathhouse.

Temperature

Three temperature scales are in general use:

  • Fahrenheit (F); water freezes at 32 °F and boils at 212 °F.
  • Celsius or centigrade1 (C); water freezes at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C.
    1
    Celsius is now the name used for centigrade temperature; it has a more modern standardization but differs from the old centigrade scale by less than 0.1°. WW-test notes that this is a footnote.
  • Kelvin or absolute (K); water freezes at 273 K and boils at 373 K.

All molecular motion ceases at about –459 °F = –273 °C = 0 K, a temperature called absolute zero. Kelvin temperature is measured from this lowest possible temperature, and it is the temperature scale most often used in astronomy. Kelvins have the same value as centigrade or Celsius degrees, since the difference between the freezing and boiling points of water is 100 degrees in each. (Note that we just say “Kelvins,” not Kelvin degrees.)

On the Fahrenheit scale, the difference between the freezing and boiling points of water is 180 degrees. Thus, to convert Celsius degrees or Kelvins to Fahrenheit degrees, it is necessary to multiply by 180/100 = 9/5. To convert from Fahrenheit degrees to Celsius degrees or Kelvins, it is necessary to multiply by 100/180 = 5/9.
