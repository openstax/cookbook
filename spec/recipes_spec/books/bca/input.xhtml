Computing for Use in the Workplace

The rise of computers in the workplace came out of a need to manage a massive quantity of numbers. The early computers were developed to essentially be “data crunchers.” You might be surprised to discover that the origins of computers date back to the 1800s in France. Joseph Marie Jacquard developed a machine to automatically weave designs in fabric using a punch card system ([link]). This punch card system laid the foundation for more advancements in number calculations for the 1880 census in the United States developed by the future founder of IBM, Herman Hollerith. IBM was founded in 1924 as Hollerith continued to improve upon the initial punch card system.

The first computer, Z1, was designed and built in the late 1930s by German Konrad Zuse. This machine was motor-driven, programmable, and contained many of the internal components that are still used today. This unit weighed over 2,000 lbs.! The Z1 paved the way for other inventors to make their mark on the new concept. At the same time, the Z1 was being developed, Hewlett-Packard emerged from a garage workspace and was founded in 1939. David Packard and Bill Hewlett worked out of a garage in California and initially worked on developing equipment for engineers. Between the years of 1940-1955 several scientists and innovators created advancements towards making the computers we use today.

Most notable was the 1943 invention of the ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Calculator) built by two professors at the University of Pennsylvania. This massive unit used vacuum tubes and shifted the architecture of computers away from mechanical processes. It is considered the forerunner for the digital computers of today. Other innovations included things like solving equations simultaneously, the invention of the transistor, and the development of the computer language. The computer language was developed by computer scientist Grace Hopper to use words instead of numbers in programming. She was a pioneer in her field not only because of her contributions, but also because she was a female mathematician and United States Navy Admiral which was unusual for the time.

These early innovations dating back to the 19th century have completely transformed not only the workplace, but our personal lives as well. Some key impacts to the workplace include:

  • Improved efficiency and productivity
  • Reduction of errors
  • Improved database management and analytics
  • Advanced communication capabilities
  • Telecommuting
  • Enhanced graphics and marketing
  • New organizational structures and departments (i.e., IT departments)

These computing machines along with the emergence and subsequent explosion of the internet, have forever transformed both our work and home lives.

One such computer was brought to the market by Xerox. This revolutionary machine, the Xerox Alto, had the ability to not only act as a calculator, but could print documents and send emails. The machine also included a screen, mouse, and keyboard. These early personal computers set the stage for the quick expansion of computing in the workplace.

Two key players in the personal computer market emerged during the 1970s. In 1975, Paul Allen and Bill Gates founded Microsoft to focus on software for the new computers. Then in 1976, Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak found Apple. With the founding of Apple, the Apple I computer with a single circuit board emerged on the market. By 1980 the computing capabilities had expanded to include color graphics, spreadsheets, and word processing programs. The market competition between Microsoft, HP, IBM, Apple, and others accelerated the technological advancements and shaped the industry and our society. In fact, in 1982, People Magazine named the computer as the “Machine of the Year” instead of their traditional “Man of the Year” recognition. Since the 1980s, computers have evolved into laptops, cellphones, and other similar devices.

Computing for Personal Use

Computers were first used in the workplace in the 1950s. Their use at that time was for scientific and engineering applications. They were designed to be calculating machines to facilitate data analysis. In 1964, Programma 101, an Italian desktop-sized programmable calculator was the first commercially viable product to hit the market. It was heavy and expensive (about the cost of one month’s commercial property rent at the time). So, although Programma 101 and a few others were commercially available in the 1960s, it really was not until 1975 that computers took a prominent position in the workplace. Prior to this time, only large corporations and research institutions had the space and resources to utilize the computers that were commercially available. The development of the microcomputer changed the face of the industry. This was prompted by the invention of the microchip by Intel in 1970 that was quickly followed by the floppy disk developed by IBM engineers and ethernet connection capability by Xerox. By 1980, there were several microcomputers/personal computers ([link]) on the market that brought computing into even the small business industry.

Almost in parallel to the evolution of computing in the workplace, computers migrated into individual’s homes. In the early 80s personal computers (PCs) were made available to the average consumer through outlets such as Sears and Radio Shack. In 1981, IBM introduced Acorn, which included Microsoft software and an Intel microchip. Soon to follow was Apple’s MacIntosh computer. Many of these new designs were streamlined and simple to use for the whole family. Also, the price point for the computers made them more attainable for the consumer with most priced well below $2,000 (but still an expensive investment for that time).

Initially these home computers were centered on gaming and entertainment. Even with rudimentary graphics and text-based games, the trend quickly caught on. Commonly played games such as chess were translated into the computer environment. These games allowed the user to experience the computer’s capabilities beyond the workplace. Tandy (Radio Shack) introduced a new computer that was much cheaper and included new features such as cassette disk storage and programming capability. The TRS-80 microcomputer sold for less than $400 and added to the competition in the industry. The computer was so successful that a newer version was developed and introduced to the market just two years later. Commodore introduced the C64 in 1982 and it still holds the record for as the highest selling computer model in the world. Tandy would later follow with the Tandy 1000 that was marketed directly to the home consumer. It is interesting to note that Tandy kept all manufacturing in the United States. Many of its competitors shifted manufacturing overseas to reduce costs and that enabled the computers to be more affordable to the average consumer.

By the mid to late 1980s the market continued to grow, and manufacturing costs decreased with the increased innovations in the industry. Computers evolved into home workstations with capabilities beyond gaming. But the trend beyond entertainment seemed slow to catch on. Many home users did not see the value in owning a personal computer. In 1987, less than 20% of the homes owned a computer. With the expansion of the internet and improved interfaces that were less technical for the average user, the industry exploded in the late 90s and early 2000s. Apple introduced customizable products during this time that allowed the buyer to select casing color schemes. Home workstations became the place to keep family finances, recipes, word processors for school research reports, and a connection to friends and family through email. In 1999 the term “Wi-Fi” was coined as more users began to connect to the internet without wires. Since 2000, the warp speed of innovations has brought to the market things such as laptops weighing less than 3 lbs., new operating systems, web browsers and social networking sites, and quantum computers. Many of today’s home computing devices are laptops that are less than 1 inch thick, equipped with high-speed connectivity, stellar graphics, and touchscreen capabilities.

Technology Today

In the healthcare industry, numerous examples of the use of computing technology are evident. Most practices (even smaller offices) and hospitals utilize electronic medical records. These records have increased the efficiency and accuracy of record management and have also increased the transparency of information to patients, families, and their care providers. For some time now, robotics and computerized systems have been used in existing surgical procedures. Managerial Decision-Making Process
  1. Identify the objective or goal, which for a business is typically maximizing income or minimizing costs.
  2. Identify alternative courses of action that can achieve a goal or solve a problem that is hindering reaching a goal.
  3. Perform a comprehensive (incremental) differential analysis of potential solutions. This includes identifying all costs, benefits and other financial and qualitative effects.
  4. Decide, based upon the analysis, the best course of action.
  5. Review, analyze and evaluate the results of the decision.

The first two step of the decision-making process is to identify the goal. In the decisions discussed in this chapter, as with many business decisions, the goal is either to maximize income or minimize costs. The second step, which is to identify the alternative courses of action will also be clear for our decisions. Both steps one and two may require more thought and research than is apparent from the analyses in this chapter, but those types of discussions are left for future accounting and management courses. Our focus in this chapter will be on steps three and four which involve determining the appropriate elements of information for the decision being made and using that information in a proper analysis so an informed decision can be reached. Step five, which involves reviewing and evaluating the decision, is briefly addressed with each type of decision we analyze.

Up to this point, your study of managerial accounting has primarily been about classifying and tracking costs in certain types of environments. Revenues have been minimally addressed as emphasis has been on the ability of a company to monitor and control costs. Control of revenues is less viable since it is difficult to make a customer buy our product. However, as stated in the first step of the decision-making process, maximizing income is usually one of the goals of an organization. Therefore, in making some short-term decisions, both costs and revenues must be analyzed.

In carrying out step three of the managerial decision-making process, an incremental analysis of potential solutions is performed. What does this involve? First, it is important to understand there are many types of short-term decisions that a business may face, but these decisions always involve choosing between alternatives. Examples of these types of decisions include accepting a special order; making a product or component versus buying the product or component; performing additional processing on a product; keeping versus eliminating a product or segment; or taking on a new project. In each of these situations, the relevant costs and the relevant revenues of one alternative should be compared to the relevant costs and relevant revenues of the other alternative(s). Therefore, an important step in the incremental analysis of potential solutions, is identifying the relevant costs and relevant revenues of the decision.

What does it mean for something to be relevant? In the context of decision making, an item is relevant if it will influence the decision being made. For example, suppose you have two options for a summer job—either flagging traffic for a road crew or working for a landscaping company doing lawn care. For either job, you will be required to have industrial grade sound protectors (plugs or headphones) for your ears. This cost would not be relevant because it is the same under either alternative and therefore will not influence your decision between the two jobs. However, if you are required to have steel-toed boots for the road work job but can wear any type of work-boot for the landscaping job, you would need to consider the difference between the costs of these two types of boots. This difference in cost between the two pairs of boots would be designated as an incremental cost and would be relevant to your decision. You believe your transportation costs will be the same for either job, thus this would also be an irrelevant cost. There can also be differences in revenues. These are known as incremental revenues. If both jobs pay the same hourly wage, but the road crew job pays overtime for any time worked over 40 hours, then this overtime wage has the potential to be an incremental revenue if overtime is a likely occurrence. When we only look at the incremental differences, this is known as incremental analysis. In conducting these types of analyses between alternatives, our initial focus will be on the quantitative aspects of the analysis, or in other words, what do the numbers tell us. However, in decision making it is important to consider qualitative issues as well. For example, in the above summer job scenario, qualitative factors may include the environment in which you would be working (road dust and tar odors, versus pollen and mower exhaust fumes), the amount of time exposed to the sun, the people with whom you will be working (some of the workers are friends already versus making new friends), and weather related issues (does either job cancel for the day due to weather). In making short-term decisions, a business will want to analyze both qualitative and quantitative factors.

In short-term decision making, revenues are often easier to evaluate than costs and typically there is only one revenue per alternative whereas there are typically many costs to consider for each alternative. How do we know if a cost will have an impact on the decision being made? The starting point is to understand the various labels that are attached to costs in these decision-making environments.

Management must determine if a cost is avoidable or unavoidable because only avoidable costs are relevant for decision-making purposes. An avoidable cost is one that can be eliminated (in whole or in part) by choosing one alternative over another. An unavoidable cost is one that does not change, or go away, if the other alternative is chosen. Variable costs are avoidable costs since variable costs do not exist if the product is no longer made, or if the portion of the business (such as a segment of division) which generated the variable costs ceases to operate (see [link].

Fixed costs, on the other hand, may be unavoidable, partially unavoidable and only in certain circumstances are they avoidable. Recall that fixed costs tend to remain constant for a period and are not easily eliminated in the short-run. Therefore, most fixed costs are unavoidable. If a fixed cost is specific only to one of the alternatives, then that fixed cost may be avoidable. Avoidable costs are future costs, not costs that have already been incurred and are therefore relevant to decision making. Sunk costs, on the other hand, are past costs and are never an avoidable cost.

Other Useful Functions

A sunk cost cannot be avoided because it has already been incurred. A sunk cost will not change regardless of which alternative management chooses. This means that sunk costs are not relevant in decision-making as they have no bearing on future events and therefore should be excluded in decision making. The basic premise sounds simple enough but sunk costs are difficult to ignore due to human nature and are sometimes incorrectly included in the decision-making process. For example, suppose you have an old car, a hand-me-down from your grandmother, and last year you spent $1,600 on repairs and new tires and were just told by your mechanic that the car needs $1,200 in repairs to operate safely. Your goal is to have a safe and reliable car. Your alternatives are to get the repairs completed or trade in the car for a newer used car. From a quantitative perspective, you have gathered the following information to help with your decision. The trade-in value of your old car will be the minimum given by the dealer or $200. The newer used car will require you to make monthly payments of $150 for two years. You are faced with two alternatives: repair the existing car or trade it in for a newer used car. What costs do you consider? Remember, the $1,600 you have already spent (note the past tense) is a sunk cost—it is a consequence of a past decision. In this example, the relevant costs for each alternative are the $1,200 in current repair costs for the alternative to keep your current car, and $1,800 minus $200 trade in or $1,600 where the $1,800 is the 24 monthly payments of $150 for the alternative to buy a newer used car. Obviously qualitative factors would be considered such as the sentimental value of your grandmother’s car, shown in [link].

Sunk costs are most problematic for business decisions when they pertain to existing equipment. The book value of an asset (historical cost – accumulated depreciation) is a sunk cost regardless of whether the asset it kept or disposed of in some manner. The cost of the asset occurred in the past and is therefore sunk and irrelevant to the decision at hand. Mangers may be reluctant to ignore sunk costs when making decisions, especially if the prior decision to purchase the asset was an unwise one. Often, when a path of action is taken by management, and that action is not achieving the desired results, managers may continue the same path in hope that the effect of prior decisions will improve. The key word in the previous sentence is “prior” which is a key indicator that information is non-relevant to a current decision. Hanging on to old decisions or old commitments is common as to do otherwise forces management to admit a bad decision was made.

Three temperature scales are in general use:

  • Celsius or centigrade[footnote] (C); water freezes at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C.

All molecular motion ceases at about –459 °F = –273 °C = 0 K, a temperature called absolute zero. Kelvin temperature is measured from this lowest possible temperature, and it is the temperature scale most often used in astronomy. Kelvins have the same value as centigrade or Celsius degrees, since the difference between the freezing and boiling points of water is 100 degrees in each. On the Fahrenheit scale, the difference between the freezing and boiling points of water is 180 degrees. Thus, to convert Celsius degrees or Kelvins to Fahrenheit degrees, it is necessary to multiply by 180/100 = 9/5. To convert from Fahrenheit degrees to Celsius degrees or Kelvins, it is necessary to multiply by 100/180 = 5/9.
