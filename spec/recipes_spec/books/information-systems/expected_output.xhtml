About Foundations of Information Systems

Foundations of Information Systems is intended to be a high-quality, fundamentals text that provides students with foundational knowledge of global information systems while preparing them to engage with more complex problems and digital technologies. The IS resource appeals to multiple audiences of learners and instructors teaching courses in information technology and those teaching in a comprehensive program in any specialty, including health information systems and business information systems. The book is designed to closely align with international standards and real-life skills needed by employers, while providing a scholarly perspective that encourages students to explore the digital world from a systems design perspective. For faculty and institutions, the e-text provides a clear set of outcomes linked to industry standards and easily complies with specialty accreditation standards.

Coverage and Scope

Foundations of Information Systems is designed to provide students with the ability to understand the concepts of IS, including hardware, software, database management systems, and data networks. The book is based on the ACM/IEEE/AIS curriculum standards for information systems (IS2020) that allows institutions to use the content for the purposes of accreditation for ABET, AACSB, and ACBSP. The openly licensed resource is grounded in concepts that cross both functional and operational areas to develop student knowledge in transactional, decisional, and collaborative business processes. Specifically, students will be able to understand and apply basic concepts associated with the collection, processing, storage, distribution, and value of information—and how IS professionals provide support to management, customers, and suppliers of the enterprise. Driven by competencies that correlate to knowledge, skills, and dispositions, the book is an asset for 2-year and 4-year information systems programs and to use in general education courses on computing.

Foundations of Information Systems consists of 12 chapters. As previously noted, the content is presented as prescribed in the IS2020 Curriculum from ACM/IEEE and ASIS. This format is in a logical order that builds on concepts and technologies from chapter to chapter, and as a result, produces better depth of understanding and application, and promotes better long-term retention. This type of organization employs a scaffolding pedagogical strategy that appeals to learners and promotes constructive learning. Using the prescribed curriculum with these pedagogical features allows the student to develop a deeper understanding of complex operations while connecting concepts to the technology and business practices in their everyday lives. This format provides professors with a logical presentation of the content, and the supplemental materials provide hands-on activities and assessments that can be easily adapted to courses and labs.

Foundations of Information Systems begins with an overview of hardware, software, and system identification, and ends with ethical considerations in using such technology as machine learning, artificial intelligence, and other newly developed technologies.

Foundations of Information Systems opens with a chapter on lifespan psychology and major theories and basic science of human development. The first chapter also introduces research methods used in the field. Subsequent chapters address the lifespan from prenatal development to death. Each chapter will discuss key developmental milestones and the wide range of variance in human development as influenced by genetics, culture, environment, and other factors. For each developmental stage, one chapter covers physical and cognitive aspects, while a second chapter covers social and emotional aspects. The final chapter discusses end of life, death, and grieving.

Scope

The scope includes the deliverables, objectives, and requirements. Project managers employ techniques like statements of work and requirements gathering to establish a clear understanding of what the project entails. Scope verification and control processes are also implemented to manage changes and ensure alignment with stakeholder expectations (PMBOK 7th edition, Chapter 5). Control processes would mean setting up a change management process so you can keep track of the scope and the budget for these changes.

The PMBOK refers to the process of managing scope as Project Scope Management. Scope is one of the five areas that can impact the entire project and needs closely monitoring. For our purposes we will define scope in accordance with the PMBOK, 7th edition:

Scope, the work performed to deliver a product, service, or result with the specified features and functions. The term project scope is sometimes viewed as including product scope. As with each area of a project, project scope management has a process that guides project managers to manage and control scope.

As a project manager your most important job is to minimize scope creep. Scope creep is where the scope of the project grows as requirements are changed or modified. It is a good idea to set up processes to help manage the scope. One of those processes is to ensure you get sign-off on a scope document before the project begins and the other is to set up a proper procedure for stakeholders to change or modify the scope, called a change management process as we defined earlier Figure 1.37.

[H2]Temperature

Three temperature scales are in general use:

  • Fahrenheit (F); water freezes at 32 °F and boils at 212 °F.
  • Celsius or centigrade1 (C); water freezes at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C.
    1
    Celsius is now the name used for centigrade temperature; it has a more modern standardization but differs from the old centigrade scale by less than 0.1°. WW-test notes that this is a footnote.
  • Kelvin or absolute (K); water freezes at 273 K and boils at 373 K.

All molecular motion ceases at about -459°F=-273°C=0K-459°F=-273°C=0K, a temperature called absolute zero. Kelvin temperature is measured from this lowest possible temperature, and it is the temperature scale most often used in astronomy. Kelvins have the same value as centigrade or Celsius degrees, since the difference between the freezing and boiling points of water is 100 degrees in each. (Note that we just say “Kelvins,” not Kelvin degrees.)

On the Fahrenheit scale, the difference between the freezing and boiling points of water is 180 degrees. Thus, to convert Celsius degrees or Kelvins to Fahrenheit degrees, it is necessary to multiply by 180/100=9/5180/100=9/5. To convert from Fahrenheit degrees to Celsius degrees or Kelvins, it is necessary to multiply by 100/180=5/9100/180=5/9.
